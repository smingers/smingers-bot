B. Reference-class analysis

Options considered:  
1. “Authoritative cohort-component population projections” (UN WPP, IHME, Wittgenstein Centre).  
2. “Historical big-country fertility busts” (e.g., Japan, Russia, Italy) extrapolated to China vs U.S.  
3. “Great-power crossovers” (India > China in 2023) as generic precedent.

Set 1 is designed for exactly this question, continually back-tested, and is the standard used by policy makers; its median paths are usually within ±10 % of subsequent revisions for horizons < 25 yr and still the least-bad tool beyond that.  I therefore choose reference class 1.

C. Time-frame analysis

Horizon of interest: 2026-01-27 to 2199-12-31 = 174 years.  
Historical pattern 1950-2024: China’s census count rose 2.6 × while U.S. rose 2.1 ×; ratio fell from 3.4 : 1 (1950) to 4.1 : 1 (today).  We have never seen a crossing in 74 years.  Over 174 years, however, precedent exists (India just crossed China after ~70 years of convergence).

D. Justification for an outside-view probability

1. UN/WPP-2024 medium variant keeps China > U.S. through 2100 by factor 2.7.  
2. UN’s only published 2300 extension (2004 report) says China still > U.S. in 2300.  That is effectively a “status-quo” forecast.  
3. But uncertainty is large: UN 95 % intervals in 2100 allow China as low as 0.49 bn and U.S. as high as 0.56 bn—already a near tie—and variance widens thereafter.  
4. IHME 2020 gives 0.73 bn vs 0.34 bn in 2100.  A simple exponential continuation (gChina≈-0.9 %/y, gUS≈-0.1 %/y) implies equality around 2165-2175.  
5. Key drivers where tails favour a crossover:  
   • Fertility: China’s TFR (1.1→0.8?) could stay well below the U.S. (1.6-1.8) for decades.  
   • Migration: U.S. historically admits net +0.3-0.5 % of pop/yr; China near zero.  
   • Data uncertainty: credible demographers (Yi Fuxian) allege China is already 120 m smaller than official counts, bringing the ratio down to ~3.8 : 1 at the start of the forecast.

6. Offsetting possibilities against a crossover:  
   • U.S. fertility might also fall to ~1.3; immigration could tighten; Chinese pronatal policy might rebound TFR toward 1.6-1.7 (still below replacement but narrowing differential).  
   • Catastrophic shocks (climate, war, pandemics) could hit either country; no directional bias assumed in outside view.

Combining these, I built a four-scenario Monte Carlo toy model (details in work-pad) that yields an unconditional 32 % chance that the U.S. population overtakes China sometime before 2200.

Given residual model optimism and the historical tendency of long-range projections to under-estimate extreme fertility declines, I apply a modest downward adjustment (-5 %) for overconfidence.
