### (b) Reference class analysis (base rates)

Plausible reference classes for “Republican advantage after the 2026 midterms”:

1) **All US midterm House outcomes since WWII**  
   - Pros: Big sample; captures “president’s-party midterm penalty.”  
   - Cons: Overstates swings versus today (stronger partisanship, fewer true swing seats, incumbency advantages, gerrymandering).

2) **Modern polarized era midterms (roughly 1994–2022)**  
   - Pros: Better match to today’s nationalization and map entrenchment.  
   - Cons: Small sample (8 midterms), includes a couple of huge-wave elections that may or may not be repeatable.

3) **Midterms when the president’s party holds the House by a small margin (say <10 seats)**  
   - Pros: Closest to the “knife-edge” environment implied by today’s narrow margin; translates directly into likely control outcomes.  
   - Cons: Very small and idiosyncratic sample; hard to generalize.

**Most suitable primary reference class:** **(2) modern era midterms** with a **cross-check** against (1) for tail risk (waves still happen).

Key base-rate takeaway: modern midterms still sometimes generate **20–40 seat** swings (2010, 2018), but can also be small (2022). So the distribution must be wide enough to allow a wave, even if the central tendency is modest.
