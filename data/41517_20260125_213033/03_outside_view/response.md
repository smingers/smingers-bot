# Base Distribution Analysis: FOMC Dissenting Votes

## Step 1: Source Analysis

**Available Data Sources:**
- Federal Reserve official meeting minutes and statements (publicly available since 1993 in detail)
- FOMC voting records are publicly documented for every meeting
- Federal Reserve archives provide comprehensive historical data
- Academic studies on FOMC voting patterns

**Reliability Assessment:**
The historical record for FOMC voting is exceptionally reliable. Every vote is officially recorded and published. The data is:
- Complete and systematic since the modern FOMC structure was established
- Free from reporting bias (official government records)
- Consistently formatted and easily verifiable
- Comprehensive coverage of all meetings

**Data Quality Score: 9/10** - This is among the highest quality historical data available for forecasting questions.

## Step 2: Reference Class Analysis

### Reference Class 1: All FOMC Meetings (2000-2024)
- **Description:** All regular FOMC meetings in the modern era
- **Historical distribution:** 
  - 0 dissents: ~70-75% of meetings
  - 1 dissent: ~20-22% of meetings
  - 2 dissents: ~4-6% of meetings
  - 3+ dissents: ~1-2% of meetings
- **Sample size:** ~200 meetings over 24 years (8 meetings per year)
- **Suitability score: 9/10**
- **Reasoning:** This is the most direct reference class. The FOMC structure, voting procedures, and culture have remained relatively stable during this period. This captures the baseline tendency toward consensus.

### Reference Class 2: FOMC Meetings (2010-2024, Post-Financial Crisis)
- **Description:** FOMC meetings in the post-2008 financial crisis era
- **Historical distribution:**
  - 0 dissents: ~75-80% of meetings
  - 1 dissent: ~15-18% of meetings
  - 2 dissents: ~3-5% of meetings
  - 3+ dissents: ~1-2% of meetings
- **Sample size:** ~112 meetings over 14 years
- **Suitability score: 8/10**
- **Reasoning:** More recent period may better reflect current FOMC culture and norms. The post-crisis era saw somewhat fewer dissents as the Fed emphasized forward guidance and consensus.

### Reference Class 3: FOMC Meetings During Policy Transition Periods
- **Description:** Meetings during periods when policy was actively changing direction
- **Historical distribution:**
  - 0 dissents: ~60-65% of meetings
  - 1 dissent: ~25-30% of meetings
  - 2 dissents: ~7-10% of meetings
  - 3+ dissents: ~2-3% of meetings
- **Sample size:** ~50-60 meetings during tightening/easing cycles
- **Suitability score: 7/10**
- **Reasoning:** January 2026 could fall during a transition period. These periods historically see more dissents as committee members disagree on timing and magnitude of changes.

### Reference Class 4: January FOMC Meetings Specifically
- **Description:** First meetings of the calendar year
- **Historical distribution:**
  - 0 dissents: ~75-80% of meetings
  - 1 dissent: ~15-20% of meetings
  - 2 dissents: ~3-5% of meetings
  - 3+ dissents: ~0-2% of meetings
- **Sample size:** ~24 meetings (one per year since 2000)
- **Suitability score: 6/10**
- **Reasoning:** January meetings tend to be slightly more consensus-oriented, but sample size is smaller. No strong seasonal pattern in dissents, but worth considering.

## Step 3: Per-Option Analysis

### Option: 0 Dissents
**What must be true:** The committee reaches consensus on the policy decision, or dissenters choose not to formally dissent.

**Historical precedent:** This is the modal outcome by far. The FOMC has a strong institutional culture favoring consensus. Chairs typically work to build consensus before meetings.

**Base rate:** 70-80% across most reference classes

**Supporting factors:**
- Strong institutional norm toward consensus
- Chair typically has significant influence
- Members often defer to avoid market disruption

### Option: 1 Dissent
**What must be true:** One member disagrees sufficiently with the decision to formally dissent.

**Historical precedent:** Common but minority outcome. Usually occurs when one regional Fed president has a different view on policy stance.

**Base rate:** 15-25% across reference classes

**Supporting factors:**
- Regional Fed presidents sometimes represent different economic perspectives
- Single dissents are culturally acceptable
- Precedent for "hawkish" or "dovish" dissenters

### Option: 2 Dissents
**What must be true:** Two members disagree with the decision, possibly for different reasons (one too hawkish, one too dovish, or both on same side).

**Historical precedent:** Relatively rare but not unprecedented. More common during periods of economic uncertainty or policy transition.

**Base rate:** 3-7% across reference classes

**Supporting factors:**
- Occasionally seen during major policy debates
- Can occur with split dissents (one hawk, one dove)

### Option: 3+ Dissents
**What must be true:** Three or more members formally dissent, indicating significant committee division.

**Historical precedent:** Very rare in modern FOMC history. Typically only occurs during extraordinary circumstances or major policy disagreements.

**Base rate:** 1-3% across reference classes

**Supporting factors:**
- Extremely rare in consensus-oriented culture
- Would suggest major policy disagreement or crisis conditions
