Analysis:
(a) Source analysis
1. FDIC Failed Bank List (updated 27 Jun 2025). Pure factual register of every insured-bank failure since 2000. High credibility; no forward-looking statements. Most recent entry 27 Jun 2025.
2. FDIC “Bank Failures in Brief” landing page. Descriptive, no 2026 data. Useful only for grand totals. Credibility high but low informational value for this forecast.
3. Bankrate article (20 Dec 2025). Journalistic summary that tallies failures through 2025. Facts (dates, assets) are sourced to FDIC; occasional opinion (“a few failures typically happen each year”). Moderate-to-high reliability.
4. San Francisco Fed Economic Letter (10 Nov 2025). Peer–reviewed research; contains one hard fact (572 failures since 2000). Other material is macro commentary, not failure counts. High credibility but little direct use.
5. Morningstar piece (13 Nov 2025). Market commentary on credit conditions. Opinions from identifiable experts (Jamie Dimon, DBRS analysts); no hard data on 2026 failures. Useful only to gauge qualitative risk; credibility moderate.
6. Liberty Street Economics post (12 Nov 2025). Fed staff research on Treasury liquidity. Gives the same cumulative failure total; otherwise macro context. High-quality but tangential.
7. Agent report (Jan 2026). Internally compiled counts of Jan-Apr failures 2001-2025 pulled from FDIC data. Method is transparent; figures spot-checked against FDIC pages. No opinions, just counts. We treat as high-quality factual data.

(b) Reference class analysis
Two candidate reference classes:
1. “All years since 2000” Jan-Apr counts (25 years). Pros: large sample, includes crisis years. Cons: dominated by 2009-2012 outliers that are unlikely to recur soon, so base rate arguably too high for current regime.
2. “Recent decade” 2016-2025 Jan-Apr counts (10 years). Pros: excludes Great-Recession clean-up, matches present regulatory/interest-rate environment. Cons: smaller N.

Because U.S. bank-fail dynamics changed markedly after 2015 (capital, liquidity, resolution rules tightened; interest-rate risk now main driver), the recent-decade class is the best fit.

(c) Timeframe analysis
Forecast horizon: 92 days remain (1 Feb–30 Apr 2026). Within our 2016-25 reference class, 26 of 30 failures (87%) that occurred in those ten years happened after mid-February, so we are effectively still in the “full season”.

(d) Justification
Empirical frequencies, 2016-2025 (Jan-Apr):
0 failures – 4/10 years = 40 %
1 failure – 2/10 years = 20 %
2 failures – 3/10 years = 30 %
3+ failures – 1/10 years = 10 %

Macro conditions for early 2026 appear neutral-to-mildly adverse:
• Fed began cutting rates in mid-2025, easing mark-to-market losses.
• Credit quality is softening (Morningstar, DBRS) but analysts still see regional banks “well-positioned”.
• No current signs of a broad deposit-run dynamic (unlike Mar 2023).

Thus the environment looks slightly better than 2020 or 2023, both of which logged two Jan-Apr failures. That nudges probability mass from “2” and “3+” toward “0” and “1”.
