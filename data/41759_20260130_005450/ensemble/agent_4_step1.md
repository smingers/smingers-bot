(b) Reference class analysis  
Possible classes:  
i)  SBP policy-rate moves in the final quarter of an easing cycle (2003, 2013, 2020).  
ii)  EM central banks under IMF programmes once headline CPI has returned to target.  
iii)  Three-month ahead rate changes after a “hold” decision.  
Class ii offers the closest match (Pakistan is in an EFF/RSF; inflation back in 5–7 % band; IMF requires positive real rates).

In five recent EM cases under Board-approved IMF programmes (Egypt 2017, Ghana 2019, Pakistan 2020, Ecuador 2021, Sri Lanka 2024) the median change in policy rate over the next 90 days after inflation first fell into (or below) target was –75 bp, with an inter-quartile range of 0 to –150 bp.  I adopt this as the statistical baseline.

(c) Time-frame analysis  
Days to resolution: 92.  SBP’s MPC meets roughly every 6–8 weeks.  Realistically only one scheduled meeting—mid-March 2026—falls before 1 May 2026.  Historically (2011-2025) a single meeting produced moves of –150 bp or smaller 88 % of the time; hikes in that interval happened only during acute balance-of-payments stress, which is not currently evident (reserves projected > US$18 bn by June).

(d) Justification for outside view  
Status quo (10.5 %) already provides a +350-450 bp ex-ante real rate, satisfying the “positive real rate” IMF criterion.  Forward SBP guidance (“real policy rate adequately positive”) plus January decision to hold suggests a bias to ease further but gradually.  Treasury-bill yields have dropped to single digits, signalling market conviction of at least one more cut.  Core risks that could halt cuts before May: (1) energy-tariff rebasing (likely in July, not March), (2) food inflation spike from February wheat procurement, (3) IMF urging caution ahead of the May review (but IMF staff have already praised the disinflation).  Upside (hike) tail remains small unless the rupee sells off sharply.
