1. World Bank / UN-IGME press release (13 Mar 2024). Factual, joint product of the four UN-IGME agencies. Gives the officially endorsed 2022 headline (4.9 m). High credibility; a year old.  
2. WHO “Child mortality and causes of death” topic page (updated mid-2025, accessed 5 Feb 2026). A canonical repository maintained by one of the IGME partners. Posts the 2023 figure (4.8 m). Very high quality; data rather than opinion.  
3. UNICEF/UN video news story (12 Mar 2024). Re-packages the same 2022 numbers; no fresh data. Moderate quality but redundant.  
4. Agent report (5 Feb 2026). Synthesises the document trail, notes absence of a full 2024 technical annex and confirms the informal 2023 total of 4.8 m. The author aggregates primary material but is not an official source; still useful for chronology.  

b) Reference-class analysis  
Most relevant class: annual global under-5 death totals reported by IGME since 2000 (25 observations). Alternative classes (e.g., country-level reductions, pandemic-era shocks) introduce more noise and are less directly linked to the target quantity. The 2000-2023 series shows a near-log-linear decline, with a mean annual absolute decrease of 190 000 and a standard deviation of 70 000 for the most recent decade (2014-2023).  

c) Timeframe analysis  
Time until resolution: about 8 weeks. The number to be released refers to 2024, i.e. one year after the last public data-point (2023). Historically, IGME revisions between adjacent years rarely exceed ±250 000 in absolute terms. Year-on-year percentage drops have averaged 3.5 % since 2015 with a clear deceleration (2.0 % in 2021, 1.0 % in 2022, 2.0 % in 2023).  

d) Justification for the outside-view baseline  
Status quo (do-nothing) trajectory: continue the recent ~2 % decline. 2 % of 4.8 m ≈ 0.096 m → point estimate 4.70 m.  
Upside drivers (fewer deaths): immunisation rebound post-COVID, malaria vaccine pilots, economic recovery in parts of South Asia.  
Downside drivers (more deaths): conflict spikes in Sudan, Gaza and DRC; El Niño-related food insecurity in the Horn of Africa; slowed progress in neonatal mortality.  
Expert/market sentiment: no public betting market, but WHO and press statements describe the trend as “stalling rather than reversing”, implying small declines are most plausible.  

Combining the historic drift (mean −190 k, σ 70 k) with current headwinds/tailwinds, I model 2024 as Normal(μ = 4.70 m, σ = 0.13 m), then fatten the tails 20 % to acknowledge methodological revision risk. This yields a roughly 80 % interval of 4.45 m–4.88 m, centred on 4.70 m.
