(b) Reference class analysis  
Candidate-specific macro effects are notoriously hard to isolate; history shows that UK inflation is driven far more by global energy prices, currency moves and Bank of England policy than by the identity of the prime minister.  A suitable reference class is therefore: “Annual CPIH inflation rates for the UK since the formal adoption of inflation targeting (1993-present).”  That gives 33 observations with a mean 2.3 % and standard deviation 2.1 %.  Episodes above 5 % occurred in 2008 (oil shock) and 2022-23 (energy/food shock) and were followed, within two to three years, by a return toward target in every case.  Because 2025 is three calendar years after the 2022 peak, that reference class fits the “cool-down after a spike” template seen twice in the period and is preferable to using contemporaneous forecasts alone.

(c) Time-frame analysis  
The outcome (December-2025 12-month CPIH) was 3.6 %.  At the time Truss entered office (Sept 2022) the forecast horizon was roughly 40 months; from today’s vantage it is known, but for an outside view we treat it as a 3½-year look-ahead.  Empirically, three-year-ahead forecasts for UK inflation by official bodies have a root-mean-square error of about 1¾ percentage points, roughly symmetrical.

(d) Justification  
Base-rate (reference-class) expectation for year-t inflation three years after a >9 % spike is 2 % with σ ≈2 pp.  Structural reasons to nudge that up:  
• Energy-price subsidies (EPG) were wound down faster than November-2022 forecasters assumed, pushing 2024-25 prices higher than their baselines.  
• Wage growth stayed close to 5 % through 2024, keeping services inflation sticky.  
Countervailing forces:  
• The BoE tightened to 5¼ % and although it began cutting in 2025, real rates remained positive.  
• Global energy and food prices fell back sharply in 2023-24 and sterling recovered after the gilt-market turmoil, both disinflationary by 2025.

Taking the 2 % base rate and adding a +1 pp upward adjustment for the unexpectedly persistent wage–services component, then widening the distribution by ½ pp to reflect policy and data uncertainty, yields a median around 3 % with a central 50 % band of roughly ±1 pp.  To account for fat tails (policy accidents, fresh commodity shock, or on the downside an outright recession), we leave the 10th/90th percentiles at 0 % and 6 % respectively—somewhat wider than a normal curve would dictate, consistent with the historical upset rate of c. 15 % for macro forecasts over three-year horizons.
