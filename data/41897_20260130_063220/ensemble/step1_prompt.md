# Summary of BBC Article: "Ukraine nuclear plant: How risky is stand-off over Zaporizhzhia?"

**Date:** November 21, 2022

## Key Facts and Objective Information:

1. **Plant specifications:** Zaporizhzhia houses six reactors and is Europe's biggest nuclear power plant. Built in the 1980s with a containment building 1.75m (5.75 ft) thick of heavily reinforced concrete on a seismic bed.

2. **Power line status (as of late August 2022):** The plant was temporarily disconnected from Ukraine's grid on August 25, 2022, when fires twice brought down its last remaining 750 kilovolt power line. The other three power lines were put out of action during the war.

3. **Backup power:** In the August incident, electricity was sourced to a less powerful line from a nearby coal-fired thermal power station, and diesel generators were also used.

4. **Reactor status (as of November 2022):** Only two of the six reactors were operating at the time of this article; the other four were in cold shutdown.

5. **Historical context:** Russia seized the plant at the start of the war. Both sides have accused each other of repeatedly shelling it.

## Expert Opinions (Named Sources):

**Rafael Grossi (IAEA head):**
- Warned of a "very real risk of nuclear disaster"
- Led a team to inspect the plant

**Mark Wenman (Head of Centre for Doctoral Training in Nuclear Energy Futures):**
- Rejects comparisons with Chernobyl or Fukushima
- States the containment building "takes a hell of a lot to breach"
- Believes diesel generators should be able to cool the reactor if grid power is lost, provided they're in good shape
- Identifies the human factor (chronic fatigue/stress of staff) as the biggest risk of nuclear accident: "And that violates all the safety principles"
- Praises Ukrainian staff for reducing the number of operating reactors

**Prof. Claire Corkhill (University of Sheffield, professor of nuclear material degradation):**
- States a pump/generator failure could lead to reactor core overheating, though "that wouldn't be as serious as Chernobyl"
- Believes "the risk of something going wrong is genuine"
- Notes Russia would be at risk just as much as Central Europe
- Expresses concern that stressed staff may not be "on top form" if something goes wrong

**Prof. Iztok Tiselj (University of Ljubljana, chair of nuclear engineering):**
- Believes "the risk of a major radioactive incident is minimal" since only two of six reactors were operating
- States "From the standpoint of European citizens there's no reason to worry"
- Believes any release from damaged spent fuel would be "so small as to be negligible"

**Lt Gen Igor Kirillov (Russia's nuclear protection corps):**
- Says shelling has damaged the plant's support systems
- Warns pump and generator failure could lead to reactor core overheating and plant facilities being destroyed

## Risk Assessment from Article:

- **Most worrying risk:** Loss of power supply to nuclear reactors combined with diesel backup generator failure, leading to loss of coolant and fuel melting
- **Less serious risks:** Damage to spent fuel pools or dry cask storage (would release radioactivity but less serious than loss of coolant)
- **Containment building damage:** Considered less likely due to robust construction

## Other Information:

- Ukraine's nuclear agency warned that diesel generators "do not provide a long-term solution"
- Plant staff working under Russian occupation under significant stress; two workers told BBC of daily kidnapping risk
- UN Secretary General called on Russia in August to pull troops out and demilitarize the area; Russia refused
- Employee letter warned of disaster if Russia tries to shut down the plant to reconnect it to Crimea instead of Ukraine
