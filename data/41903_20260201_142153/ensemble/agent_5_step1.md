(b) Reference class analysis  
Candidates:  
1. “Russia–Ukraine conflict” itself (2014-2026): 2 bilateral cease-fire agreements (Minsk I & II) reached over 26 quarters ⇒ raw base-rate ≈ 5 % chance that any given calendar quarter produces an agreement.  
2. Interstate wars since 1945 lasting ≥ 3 years (Korean War, Iran-Iraq, Ethiopia-Eritrea, etc.): historical war-termination studies (Walter 2002; Fortna 2018) find an annual hazard rate of 15-25 % for a negotiated stop to hostilities after year 3.  Translated to a 3-month window: 4-8 %.  
3. All modern wars with great-power involvement and territorial dispute (n≈25): annual cease-fire/armistice probability roughly 12 % (Paul Diehl data set) ⇒ 3-month window ≈ 3-4 %.  

Suitability: Class 1 uses the same belligerents, same incentive structure and the same bargaining obstacles (territory, neutrality).  It is therefore chosen as the primary reference class; the others serve as cross-checks.

(c) Time-frame analysis  
• Today → Resolution deadline: 90 days.  
• During the prior 4 years of full-scale invasion (16 quarters) zero qualifying bilateral cease-fires were reached; during the earlier 2014-15 phase two were reached in two successive quarters.  Empirically, the probability conditional on “no cease-fire in the last 15 quarters” appears lower than the long-run 5 % quarter-rate.  
• Negotiations often exhibit clustering: both Minsk deals happened amid rapid Russian advances when Kyiv needed an operational pause.  Current frontline is comparatively static; neither side is signalling desperation.  That historical pattern further lowers the near-term base-rate.

(d) Justification  
1. Structural obstacles identified by BBC, Harvard and the Agent report (territory, sequencing, security guarantees) remain unresolved in the Paris process.  
2. Moscow’s published June 2025 memorandum still demands Ukrainian withdrawal and neutrality; Kyiv’s January 2026 statements insist on NATO-style guarantees first.  Positions are mutually exclusive.  
3. External pressure is limited.  Paris meeting created only a “declaration of intent”; no party has tabled the kind of concessions (e.g., sanctions relief for Russia, territorial compromise by Ukraine) that accompanied Minsk I & II.  
4. Time is short.  Even if talks restart tomorrow, drafting, translating, domestic sign-off and coordination of monitors normally take several weeks (compare Minsk II: 17 days from sketch to signature).  Any further slip in the calendar rapidly pushes signature past the 1 May deadline.  
5. Cross-conflict data give a 3-month cease-fire probability of roughly 3-8 %.  The Russia-Ukraine specific base-rate is ≈ 5 %, and contemporary conditions (harder lines, no breakthrough on guarantees) look less favourable than in 2014-15.  
6. Adjustments:  
   • +2 pp for unusually intense third-party shuttle diplomacy (U.S.–EU “Coalition of the Willing”, Turkish venue ready, daily contact channels functioning).  
   • –3 pp for hardened bargaining positions and front-line equilibrium (no obvious incentive shock).  
   Net adjustment ≈ –1 pp.
