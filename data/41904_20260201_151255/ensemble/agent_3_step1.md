## (b) Reference class analysis

I considered three reference classes:

1. **Hungary parliamentary elections since 2010 (N=4)**  
   - Outcome: Fidesz won a seat plurality 4/4, and typically a large one.  
   - Strength: Same country, same dominant party, broadly same regime characteristics.  
   - Weakness: Small N, and this cycle plausibly differs because a new opposition vehicle (Tisza) appears unusually strong.

2. **Dominant-party incumbents in “tilted playing-field” democracies (broadly Europe/Central & Eastern Europe) facing unified/credible challengers**  
   - Typical pattern: incumbents often retain plurality even when unpopular, due to media/state resources and electoral rules; but losses do occur, especially amid economic malaise and opposition consolidation.  
   - Strength: Captures structural advantage patterns.  
   - Weakness: Heterogeneous institutions; hard to translate into a precise base rate.

3. **New parties (formed within ~1–2 years) attempting to beat an entrenched incumbent in seats**  
   - Typical pattern: many surge then fall short; organizational depth and candidate quality matter disproportionately in SMC-heavy or mixed systems.  
   - Strength: Directly relevant to Tisza’s “new party” risk.  
   - Weakness: Depends strongly on electoral system and whether the new party is truly “new” vs a rebrand of existing structures.

**Most suitable composite:** (1) as the anchor for Hungary-specific baseline, adjusted using (3) to penalize “new party execution risk,” and (2) to reflect that dominant parties sometimes do lose when conditions align.

## (d) Justification (outside view)

### Status quo / base-rate pull
- Fidesz has held power since 2010 and (per the agent report) **never fell below 133 seats** in the 199-seat era. That is a very strong prior that Fidesz remains the largest seat-winner.
- The electoral system is **not fully compensatory** and contains multiple pro-largest-party features (SMCs + fragment votes + districting history). This structurally raises the probability that Fidesz could still win **more seats than its vote share would suggest**, and raises the hurdle for Tisza to win *more seats than Fidesz*.

### Challenger strength vs “new party” execution risk
- Tisza is unusually credible as a challenger (cross-ideological appeal; leader is a former insider; signs of professionalization). In many countries, that would push the probability of incumbent defeat materially upward.
- But the reference class for **new parties in mixed/SMC-heavy systems** is unforgiving: even if the national vote is close, the challenger can lose many SMCs due to (i) candidate quality variation, (ii) weaker local networks, and (iii) rural/urban distribution disadvantages.

### Poll uncertainty as an “outside-view dampener”
- The polling ecosystem described is unusually bifurcated (pro- vs anti-government pollsters). In an outside view, that widens the error bars and reduces confidence that any single polling snapshot implies a seat-plurality flip.
- Given that the key question is **seat plurality**, not vote plurality, and given the system tilt, the outside view should assign a **meaningful but minority** probability to Tisza finishing with more seats than Fidesz.

**Outside-view synthesis:**  
Start from a Hungary-2010s prior near “Fidesz stays largest” (high), then discount for (i) economic malaise/anti-incumbent environment and (ii) credible unified challenger, but also discount Tisza for (iii) new-party local-network risk and (iv) SMC translation disadvantage. This yields a probability clearly above a token upset chance, but still below 50%.
