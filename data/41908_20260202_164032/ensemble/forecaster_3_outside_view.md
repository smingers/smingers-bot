### (b) Reference class analysis (outside view)

To estimate a baseline probability, the most useful reference classes are about **how often WHO declares PHEICs at all**, and **how often WHO declares PHEICs for influenza-like threats**.

**Reference class 1: “Any new WHO PHEIC declaration in a ~3‑month window.”**  
- Suitability: Good for capturing *organizational propensity* to declare PHEICs in short windows.  
- Limitation: Overinclusive; many PHEICs are unrelated to respiratory viruses.

**Reference class 2: “WHO PHEIC declarations for influenza (or influenza-pandemic-type events).”**  
- Suitability: Better match to H5N1’s nature (a respiratory virus with pandemic potential).  
- Limitation: Sample size is tiny (essentially H1N1 2009), so estimates will be noisy.

**Reference class 3: “Avian influenza A(H5) events leading to a PHEIC.”**  
- Suitability: Closest match to the exact question.  
- Limitation: Appears to be **zero historical precedent** (no H5 PHEIC to date). A pure “0 out of N” base rate would suggest an extremely low probability, but we should avoid collapsing to ~0 because the world changes and tail risks exist.

**Most suitable for an outside view baseline:** A blend of (1) and (2), with (3) acting as a strong dampener (because precedent is nil).

### (d) Justification (outside-view baseline)

**Step 1: Base rate for “any PHEIC soon.”**  
Using the agent-reported list (8 PHEIC declarations since 2009 through early 2026), the crude rate is roughly:
- **8 declarations / 17 years ≈ 0.47 per year**.  
Over **0.24 years**, expected declarations ≈ 0.47 × 0.24 ≈ **0.11**.  
If we approximate with a Poisson process, the probability of **at least one** new PHEIC in that window is:
- **1 − exp(−0.11) ≈ 10%**.

So, outside view: **~10%** that WHO declares *some* new PHEIC in the next three months.

**Step 2: Share that are “influenza-like.”**  
Historically, only **one** of the listed PHEICs is clearly influenza (H1N1 2009). That suggests influenza PHEICs are on the order of **~1/8 of PHEICs** (very rough, but it’s a tetherable figure from the provided material).  
- 10% × 12.5% ≈ **1.25%** for an influenza-type PHEIC in a random 3‑month period.

**Step 3: Conditioning on “H5N1 specifically.”**  
Avian influenza A(H5) has had repeated zoonotic events and large animal outbreaks without a PHEIC historically (per the agent report and consistent with WHO’s generally conservative PHEIC use). That pushes the baseline down. However, because this is a tail-risk domain (pandemic emergence), I wouldn’t push it to near-zero.

**Outside view synthesis:** The “any PHEIC soon” rate points to ~10% for something, but “influenza PHEIC soon” is ~1–2%, and “H5N1 PHEIC soon” is likely in that vicinity or slightly above if we allow for heightened attention to H5 spillovers—yet still low in a 3‑month window.

- **Anchor/tether:** The crude historical frequency implies ~**10%** chance of *any* PHEIC in ~3 months.  
- **Down-weight for category:** Influenza-like PHEICs appear extremely rare (only the 2009 H1N1 example in the provided timeline), bringing it to roughly **~1–2%**.
