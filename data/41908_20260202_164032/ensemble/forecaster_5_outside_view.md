(a) Source analysis  
1. RAND commentary (Dec-2024).  A policy op-ed urging the United States (not WHO) to declare a domestic public-health emergency for H5N1.  Factual elements: 61 U.S. human H5N1 cases, first severe case (Louisiana), bovine epizootic in 300+ herds, isolated Canadian & Missouri cases without animal exposure.  Opinions: authors judge U.S. response inadequate and recommend a PHE.  Quality: credible think-tank, data generally traceable to CDC/USDA, but advocacy piece; minimal direct bearing on WHO decision-making.

2. CDC historical timeline (to 1959).  Pure background; no policy or current data.  Quality high, but not decision-relevant.

3. WHO DON 5-Dec-2025 on A(H5N5).  Factual: first recorded human H5N5 death, WHO’s formal risk assessment = “Low”.  No mention of PHEIC.  High-quality primary source; demonstrates WHO’s current risk framing for H5 viruses.

4. Agent report (1-Feb-2026).  Collates WHO PHEIC history, all public WHO communications on H5 since 2023, global human-case counts, animal spread.  Identifies that (i) WHO has never convened an Emergency Committee for H5N1, (ii) eight PHEICs total since 2005, one of them an influenza (H1N1 2009).  Mixture of primary documents and reputable secondary summaries; internally consistent.

(b) Reference class analysis  
Candidate classes and suitability:  
i. All WHO PHEIC declarations 2005-2025: 8 events / 19 yrs ≈ 0.42 yr⁻¹ (good data volume but heterogenous diseases).  
ii. WHO PHEICs specifically for influenza viruses: 1 event (H1N1 2009) / 19 yrs ≈ 0.05 yr⁻¹ (small N but same pathogen family).  
iii. “Candidate diseases that had ≤100 confirmed human cases and no sustained human-to-human spread three months before decision.”  Few precedents (e.g., Zika 2016, Mpox 2022) so N tiny.  
iv. “Situations where WHO was pressured by experts/media to act but assessed risk as ‘low’.”  Analytically useful but no stable numerical base rate.

Class ii (PHEICs for influenza) best balances relevance (same family, same IHR mechanism) and statistical meaning, despite the small N; we will anchor on it and adjust.

(c) Timeframe analysis  
• Time remaining: 88 days (2 Feb → 30 Apr 2026).  
• Historically, once WHO decides a PHEIC is warranted it moves fast: Zika (first microcephaly clusters recognised Nov 2015 → PHEIC Feb 2016); COVID-19 (first WHO notice 31 Dec 2019 → PHEIC 30 Jan 2020).  Median “incubation” 1-2 months.  
• No Emergency Committee for H5N1 has even been announced.  Typically 1-2 public meetings precede declaration, so the clock would be tight.

(d) Justification  
Base rate: Using class ii, annual probability of an influenza PHEIC ≈ 5%.  A 3-month window scales that to about 1.3%.  

Adjustment factors upward  
+ Large, unusual bovine epizootic in a high-income country; 71 confirmed U.S. human infections (orders of magnitude above pre-2024 baseline).  
+ Two U.S. fatalities and a severe Canadian ICU case, suggesting rising virulence/pathogenicity.  
+ Demonstrated mammalian adaptation mutations (e.g., PB2 E627K).  
+ Growing media and expert pressure (RAND piece, Nature, Lancet editorials) could prompt WHO action.

Adjustment factors downward  
– WHO’s last formal risk assessment (Dec-2025) still “Low”; no Emergency Committee yet.  
– No evidence of sustained H-to-H transmission; clusters remain animal-exposure linked.  
– Only 88 days remain; a Committee must be convened, meet twice and recommend; leadership must endorse—administratively tight.  
– Political caution after covid-19; WHO may wait for clearer epidemiological signal before declaring another PHEIC.

Net: starting from 1-2 % baseline, multiplicative risk-inflation of roughly ×3–4 for the unusual cattle situation and policy pressure yields 4-7 %.  Short time window and administrative sequencing trims the top end slightly.  I settle on 6 %.
