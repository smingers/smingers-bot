Candidate classes:  
• Daily Google-Trends values for mid-tier U.S. House members (baseline index 3-15).

(c) Time-frame analysis  
Time between the two datapoints: 9 days (05 Feb → 14 Feb).  Historical daily volatility over 9-day gaps for the reference class clusters around ±4–5 index points.  Thus, even without a major headline, crossing the ±3 threshold is more likely than not.

(d) Justification for outside-view probabilities  
Baseline (from reference class):  
• Increase > 3 ≈ 34 %  
• Decrease > 3 ≈ 31 %  
• Within ±3 ≈ 35 %

Two generic considerations nudge these figures only slightly:  
1. Floor effect.  The 05 Feb starting value will be constrained by the 28 Jan spike already in the 30-day window; a second equally large spike before 14 Feb is statistically less common than a reversion.  That tilts a hair toward “Decrease.”  
2. Scaling artefact.  Because Google fixes the scale for 15 Jan–14 Feb, any mid-window surge (say on 8 Feb) can pull the whole scale upward, lowering both 05 Feb and 14 Feb numbers.  That marginally helps “Doesn’t change” versus “Increase.”

The adjustments are small (a few percentage points) because we have not yet used any inside-view knowledge of specific upcoming events.

Outside view calibration:

• Historical upset rate (i.e., extreme one-day spikes nobody foresaw) for political-figure searches is high but still leaves 30–40 % of periods within ±3.  
• No structural reason to expect huge asymmetry up vs. down in a 9-day window when the starting value is only 7.  
• Therefore remain close to the empirical base-rate distribution and round to 1 % precision.
