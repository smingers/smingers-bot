(b) Reference class analysis  

Candidates:  
1. All UK Westminster by-elections 2010-26 in Labour-held seats. (17 cases; Labour lost 2 → 12 % loss rate)  
2. Sub-set where pre-by-election Labour majority exceeded 20 points. (8 cases; Labour lost 1: Runcorn & Helsby 2025 → 13 % loss rate)  
3. Sub-set of (2) occurring while national polling showed Labour clearly trailing (only Copeland 2017 and Runcorn 2025; Labour lost 1/2 → 50 % loss rate, n small).  

Reference class 2 is large enough to give a baseline yet specific to “safe” seats, so I adopt it and then adjust for today’s unusually adverse national climate.

(c) Timeframe analysis  

Time to vote: 20 days.  UK by-election vote-intention tends to shift modestly in final three weeks unless a major scandal erupts. Empirically (Curtice 2015 study) median late swing is ≈ 2 pts.  Thus the outside view should not yet bake in last-minute shocks but must allow ~±2 pt noise.

(d) Justification for outside-view numbers  

Baseline from reference class 2: Labour win probability ≈ 87 %.  

Adjustment factors that push odds away from Labour:  
• National polling crash to <20 % (unprecedented for governing party within 18 months) – cut Labour chance by roughly one-third (× 0.67).  
• Workers Party withdrawal (10 % 2024 vote) likely benefits Greens more than Labour → adds non-Lab alternative path.  
• Burnham-veto row dampens Labour local enthusiasm (historical precedent: Copeland candidate parachute cost est. −4 pts).  
• Presence of high-profile Reform candidate and recent Runcorn upset demonstrate right-populist capacity to convert safe Lab seats.  

Collectively I reduce Labour probability from 87 % to about 60 %.  

Split of non-Labour remainder:  
• Reform historically the main challenger in “white-outer-ward” parts; national polling surge; precedent Runcorn.  
• Greens strongest in inner Manchester wards; WPB exit adds vote, but first-past-the-post handicaps them once Reform becomes perceived principal challenger.  
• “Other” (Con, LibDem, SDP, etc.) almost no path—combined 2024 vote 12 % and likely to be squeezed tactically.  

Heuristic share of upset outcomes: of the 40 % non-Lab bucket, allocate two-thirds Reform (≈ 27 %), one-third Greens (≈ 13 %), leave 2 % for Other.

Check against historic upset frequency: overall Labour-loss rate 12 %, but we are positing 40 %. Given the extreme, atypical context (leadership crisis, triple-way field, national polling nadir) multiplying base rate by ~3 is defensible yet still conservative relative to PollCheck’s 46 % non-Lab total.
