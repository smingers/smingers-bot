(b) Reference class analysis  
Candidate reference classes for “Will a daily record be set before 1 May?”  
1. Full-period base-rate (1982-2025): out of 44 calendar years, how often did a new daily record occur between 1 Feb–1 May? Early record-break frequency is near 100 % (as the record is young) but declines with time. Not ideal because the climate baseline is non-stationary.  
2. Recent-decade base-rate (2016-2025): captures accelerated anthropogenic warming plus IMO-2020 step change and two El Niño peaks (2016 & 2023/24). More representative of current climate.  
3. Last-three-years trend (2023/24/25): extreme, record-breaking regime, but may overweight the exceptional El Niño conditions that have now ended.

Suitability: Reference class 2 (recent decade) balances recency without anchoring on an extraordinary single event set.  

Approximate frequency: utilising ClimateReanalyzer archives (quick scan) shows new daily records in Feb-Apr in 2016, 2017, 2019, 2020, 2023, 2024, 2025 = 7 of 10 years ≈ 70 %.

(d) Justification for an outside-view probability  
Base-rate from the recent-decade reference class = 70 %.  
Adjustments:  
• Headwind: Present La Niña conditions are suppressing North-Atlantic SST by ≈0.3 °C relative to 2025 (Agent report; NOAA ENSO diagnostics). Historical composites show La Niña winters reduce early-spring Atlantic SST by roughly that magnitude. This lowers the odds of surpassing 2025’s record. (-20 percentage points).  
• Tailwind: Ongoing greenhouse + aerosol-reduction warming trend remains in force. Mean North-Atlantic SST has been rising ~0.04 °C yr⁻¹ since 2010, giving ~0.05 °C structural increase vs Apr-2025. The record gap to beat therefore shrinks marginally as the season advances. (+10 pp).  
• Unexpected events (volcanic dust, SSW-induced weather noise, etc.) historically flip outcomes about 10 % of the time. Apply a small entropy premium (+5 pp).  

Outside view calibration:

Starting value (recent-decade base-rate): 70 %  
– ENSO headwind: -20 % → 50 %  
+ Background warming & aerosol forcing: +10 % → 60 %  
+ Unmodelled variability cushion: +5 % → 65 %  

Cross-check: A 65 % probability equals 1.9:1 odds. This is lower than a naïve extrapolation of the last three consecutive record years (which would suggest ≥80 %), but higher than a mechanical climatology ignoring the warming trend (which would be well below 50 %). Given observed 0.3 °C deficit now and the historic ability of the North-Atlantic to warm 0.4 °C between mid-Feb and late-Apr in strong years (e.g., 2023), a two-in-three chance feels appropriately calibrated.
