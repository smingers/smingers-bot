(b) Reference class analysis  
Candidate reference classes:  
1. Russian/Belarusian delegations when forced to compete neutrally (OAR-2018, ROC-2022) – same countries, same “neutral” constraints conceptually.  
2. All national delegations of ≈20 athletes at recent Winter Games.  
3. Small subsets (≤25 athletes) drawn from top-five Olympic powers (e.g., Norway’s Alpine team only).  

Suitability: #1 best matches athlete quality, sports culture and some sanction effects; #2 & #3 confound by including nations with much weaker talent pipelines.  I adopt reference class #1.

(d) Justification (outside view)  
1. Scale the historical medal efficiency to a 20-athlete delegation.  
   • OAR-2018: 0.10 medals/athlete → expected 2.0 medals if efficiency unchanged.  
   • ROC-2022: 0.15 medals/athlete → expected 3.0 medals.  
   Average efficiency ≈0.125 → λ = 0.125 × 20 = 2.5 expected medals.  
2. Down-adjust for 2026 constraints:  
   - No team events (OAR/ROC earned ≈5-6 team medals each time).  
   - Rigorous vetting excluded several elite, military-affiliated stars (e.g., Aleksandr Bolshunov).  
   - One-third of normal training/competition opportunities lost due to federation bans 2022-25.  
   Heuristic haircut: reduce efficiency by roughly 45 %.  New λ ≈ 2.5 × 0.55 ≈ 1.4.  
3. Treat medal acquisitions as a Poisson process with mean λ = 1.4.  P(at least one medal) = 1 − e^(−1.4) ≈ 0.75.  
4. Expert/market anchoring: There are no published betting markets, but most mainstream previews list Adelia Petrosyan (figure skating) and Daria Nepryaeva (xc skiing) as “outside medal threats,” which is compatible with a 60-80 % range, not with extreme (>90 % or <40 %) probabilities.  
5. Upset frequency: In the last three Winter Games, 13 % of podiums featured an athlete from outside the top-ten nations, showing that single-athlete surprises are plausible.  That nudges probability slightly upward because a single brilliant Russian/Belarusian could be such a surprise even from a diminished pool.
