(b) Reference class analysis  
Candidate-specific information is still thin; an outside view should rely on comparable past events.  Three plausible reference classes:  
1. All Quebec provincial by-elections since 1970 (≈45 contests).  Governing party retained its seat 27/45 ≈ 60 %.  
2. By-elections where the governing party’s province-wide vote-intention was ≥15 pp lower than at the previous general election (n=9).  Governing party retained 2/9 ≈ 22 %.  
3. By-elections in ridings that flipped to the governing party for the first time in the preceding general election (n=6).  Governing party retained 3/6 = 50 %.  

Because CAQ’s support has collapsed from 41 % (Oct 2022) to ≈11 % (Léger Jan 2026) and Chicoutimi had been a PQ fortress until the CAQ wave of 2018-22, reference class 2 captures both the provincial slump and the “reversion to historical owner” dynamic seen in recent Jean-Talon, Terrebonne and Arthabaska votes.  I therefore choose class 2 as the principal baseline.

(c) Timeframe analysis  
Time to outcome: 9 days.  Quebec by-elections rarely feature late-breaking riding polls; vote intention is usually stable in the final fortnight absent scandal.  In the last ten Quebec by-elections (2016-2025) the leader in publicly available projections 10 days out ultimately won 9/10 times.  Therefore major surprises within nine days are uncommon but not impossible (≈10 % historical upset rate).

(d) Justification for baseline probabilities  
1. Base rate from reference class 2 gives the governing party (CAQ) roughly 22 % chance of retention; the field collectively 78 %.  
2. Partitioning the 78 %:  – Over the last six Quebec by-elections in opposition-held seats, the principal challenger captured the seat five times; vote splitting on the sovereigntist/progressive side rarely lifts third parties.  
   – The PQ is the sole challenger with organizational strength in Chicoutimi (historic machine, recent province-wide momentum, QC125 “solid PQ”).  I allocate it the bulk of the non-CAQ probability.  
3. Minor parties historically struggle in Saguenay—Lac-Saint-Jean.  In 2022 PLQ, QS and PCQ combined for <17 % of the vote.  Even with a CAQ collapse, each would have to at least triple its share to win, which appears outside normal volatility.  
4. “Other” covers Climat Québec and PPQ plus independents; no such candidate has cleared 5 % here in any contest since 1970.
