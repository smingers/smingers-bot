(b) Reference-class analysis

Candidate reference classes:

1. All 60 WTO accession applicants since 1995 – too broad; includes many stalled cases.
2. The 38 applicants that eventually acceded – still broad, but yields data on end-game timelines.
3. Accessions that had produced a Draft Working-Party Report and were aiming to sign at an upcoming Ministerial Conference – best match (≈ nine cases: Latvia 1998, Kyrgyz 1998, Bahrain 2003, Saudi Arabia 2005, Afghanistan 2015, Liberia 2016, Comoros & Timor-Leste 2024, Seychelles 2015).

Chosen class: (3).  These are countries that, like Ethiopia today, were in the “home stretch” but still needed to (i) close final bilaterals, (ii) get the Draft WP Report adopted, (iii) have the accession protocol approved at a GC/MC, (iv) ratify domestically, and (v) deposit the instrument so that membership could enter into force 30 days later.

Empirical features of class (3):
• Median time from Draft WP Report to protocol adoption: ≈ 5 months.
• Median time from protocol adoption to membership: ≈ 6 months (range 2 – 11 months).
• No historical accession had the entire “adoption-to-membership” phase in < 45 days; the fastest (Oman 2000) was 61 days.

Therefore, even for “near-finish” cases, membership in ≤ 74 days is historically rare (< 5 %).

(c) Time-frame analysis

Time remaining until the question closes: 74 days (≈ 2.4 months).

Historical pattern: once an accession protocol is adopted, 30 days must elapse after the instrument of ratification is deposited before membership takes effect.  Thus, to be a member by 1 May 2026, Ethiopia would need to:

1. Have its Final WP meeting and adopt the WP report no later than the first half of March 2026,
2. Obtain General Council or MC14 approval and sign the protocol by late March,
3. Finish domestic ratification and deposit the instrument at the WTO by ≈ 1 April 2026.

No evidence yet exists that the 7th (presumed final) WP meeting has even taken place, let alone that bilaterals are closed or that Ethiopia’s parliament has a ratification bill ready.

Offsetting factors:

+ Ethiopia and the WTO Secretariat have openly targeted MC14; political pressure is higher than average.  (Add ~2 percentage points.)
− Ethiopia still lacked a final WP meeting as of late-2025, and ratification generally takes several months even for countries with streamlined executive procedures.  (Subtract ~3 points.)
− Domestic conflict and fiscal pressures may slow legislative throughput.  (Subtract ~1 point.)
+ Ethiopia could, in principle, accelerate ratification by emergency decree; autocratic systems sometimes do.  (Add ~1 point.)

Net adjustment: 5 %  – 3 % + 3 % ≈ 5 %.

Outside view calibration:
