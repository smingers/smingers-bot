(b) Reference class analysis  

Possible classes:  
1. Dutch municipal elections since 1994 (nationwide #1 party vote share).  
2. Other Dutch second-order elections (provincial, European) top-party shares.  
3. Municipal elections in other highly proportional European countries.

Class 1 is directly on-point: same election type, identical aggregation rule, same electorate behaviour patterns (turnout ~50 %, local lists allowed).  It is therefore chosen.  Adding 1994, 1998, 2002 & 2006 (all ≥18 % for “Lokale partijen”, per academic literature) enlarges sample to 8 elections, all successes.

(d) Justification  

Empirical record: 8/8 elections ≥15 % (100 % success).  Even the lowest margin (≈18 %) exceeds the threshold by 3 pp.  

Structural drivers:  
• Fragmentation benefits local lists, but because they are aggregated into a single “Lokale partijen” line, growing fragmentation inside that bloc does not reduce the bloc total—if anything it increases it.  
• National parties individually poll in the low- to mid-teens; thus for the top line to fall below 15 %, local parties would have to collapse AND no national party could exceed 14.9 %, an extremely unlikely double contingency.  
• The only plausible failure mode is a change in reporting—e.g., Verkiezingsuitslagen ceases to aggregate “Lokale partijen”.  This has never happened in 30 years of publication and would require a conscious methodological overhaul with no sign of adoption.

Upset frequency: in Dutch elections truly unprecedented events (e.g., Fortuyn 2002, BBB 2023) occur perhaps once a decade, yet even those did not dent the local-party aggregate below 20 % in 2002 or 2022.  Assign a generous 10 % chance that either statistical or definitional surprises drop the #1 share under 15 %.
