(b) Reference class analysis  
Candidate classes:  
1. Mediterranean Q1 (Jan–Mar) deaths in previous 10 years.  
2. Ratio of deaths by 10 Feb to full Q1 in past high-mortality years.  
3. Years that ultimately exceeded 900 deaths before April (e.g., 2016).  

Class 1 is broad but many years (2018-2025) never reached 900 by Q1, making it less predictive for the current extreme pace.  
Class 2 focuses on temporal progression and is therefore best suited: in 2016 and 2025 the Feb 10 figure was about half the eventual Q1 total (≈0.50–0.55).  

Thus we adopt reference class 2: “Share of Q1 deaths already recorded by 10 February.”

(d) Justification  
1. Quantitative anchor: IOM has already logged 524 deaths/missing (through 10-Feb). This is an official, timestamped dataset figure.

3. Real-time reports (Al Jazeera 22-Feb) confirm continued incidents, so we can infer at least ~30–60 more deaths already accrued after 10 Feb.

5. Latency risk: IOM sometimes takes 3–10 days to post incidents, but the cut-off is 1 April, giving time for late-March events to be logged.  
6. Upset possibilities:  
   • Drastic weather improvement could paradoxically reduce fatalities if crossings pause, but March usually sees more voyages.  
   • A sudden EU-Libya interdiction surge could curb departures but historically shifts deaths to invisible shipwrecks that IOM eventually logs.  
   • Data-entry lag is the biggest practical threat and has occasionally delayed 5–10 % of Q1 totals past early April.  

Given those factors, the chance of failing to reach 900 is principally the combination of (i) a steep fall-off in actual deaths plus (ii) unusual IOM reporting delay. I judge that joint probability at roughly 7-10 %.
