### Electoral System Structure
- The National Assembly has **90 total members**
- **88 seats** are elected via List PR across **8 constituencies** (11 MPs each)
- Each constituency is subdivided into 11 electoral districts where each party fields one candidate; voters effectively determine list ordering rather than voting directly for a party list
- Seats are distributed using a **quota system within constituencies**, with remaining seats allocated nationally via **D'Hondt method** with a **4% threshold**

## Summary: Slovenia's National Assembly Elections Act – Seat Allocation Rules

This article from IDEA International describes the electoral system used for Slovenia's National Assembly elections, as established in the 1992 Elections Act (amended 2006).

### Key Structural Features:

- **Constituency-based proportional representation**: Deputies are elected in constituencies, balancing equal citizen representation with proportional political representation.
- **Ethnic minority seats**: The Italian and Hungarian ethnic communities each elect one dedicated deputy, separate from the general allocation.

### Seat Allocation Mechanism (Articles 90–92):

1. **Constituency-level allocation (Article 90)**: Seats are distributed using a modified quotient method — the total votes in a constituency are divided by the number of seats plus one (rounded up). Each list receives seats equal to how many times this quotient divides into its vote total.

2. **Candidate selection within lists (Article 91)**: Elected candidates are determined by their individual share of votes within electoral districts. Ties are resolved by lot.

3. **National-level top-up allocation (Article 92)**: Remaining unallocated seats (out of 88 total) are distributed nationally using the **d'Hondt system**, dividing each party's national vote total by numbers 1 through 88 and ranking quotients.
   - **4% threshold**: Lists receiving fewer than 4% of total national votes are **excluded** from seat allocation entirely.
   - The national allocation ensures proportionality by topping up parties based on the difference between their nationally-deserved seats and those already won at constituency level.
</Summary>
