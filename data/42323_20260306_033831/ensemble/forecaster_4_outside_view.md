  - Strongly affected by weather/seasonality; early-year windows are meaningfully lower than annual totals.

3. Early-year share of annual total in years with similar migration regime (2021–2025)
- Strength:
  - Good for translating annual totals into Jan-Apr expectations.
  - Seasonal pattern in Channel crossings is persistent: crossings occur year-round but intensify in warmer/calmer months, especially later spring into summer and autumn.
- Weakness:
  - Annual totals themselves vary, and the share by April 30 can shift with weather.

4. First-third-of-year arrivals in maritime irregular migration routes generally
- Strength:
  - Broad outside view for seasonality.
- Weakness:
  - Too generic; local Channel weather, French enforcement, and smuggler adaptation matter a lot.

Most suitable reference class:
- A hybrid of (2) and (3): recent UK small-boat seasons, especially 2024 and 2025, using partial-year benchmarks and known seasonality.
- This is much more suited than whole-history 2018–2020, because those years are from a different lower-flow regime.

(c) Timeframe analysis

- Today is March 6, 2026.
- Resolution asks for cumulative arrivals from Jan. 1, 2026 through Apr. 30, 2026 inclusive.
- So:
  - About 65 days of the resolution window have already elapsed.
  - About 56 days remain until Apr. 30.
  - We are forecasting a partly observed seasonal window, though the prompt does not provide the actual 2026 cumulative to date.

Historical patterns relevant to Jan-Apr:
- Crossings are highly seasonal, generally lower in winter and ramping up in spring/summer when sea conditions improve.
- The Guardian and Sky benchmarks imply:
  - By July 1, 2024: 13,489–13,574.
  - By July 1, 2025: 19,982.
- Since July 1 is 182 days into the year, these totals average:
  - 2024: about 74/day.
  - 2025: about 110/day.
- Jan-Apr is only 120 days and is more weather-constrained than May-June, so Jan-Apr should usually be materially below two-thirds of the Jan-Jul total.
- A rough seasonal conversion:
  - If Jan-Apr contributes around 50–65% of Jan-Jul in a typical year, then:
    - 2024 Jan-Apr might have been roughly 6.7k–8.8k.
    - 2025 Jan-Apr might have been roughly 10.0k–13.0k.
- That places the most plausible historical analogue range for Jan-Apr 2026 somewhere in the broad 8k–14k zone, assuming continuity with 2024–2025 and no major regime break.

This makes the option bands informative:
- 0–5,000 would require either a very weak year or extraordinary suppression/bad weather.
- 5,001–10,000 corresponds to a moderate but not especially high early-year pace.
- 10,001–20,000 seems to cover most of the plausible continuation scenarios from recent years.
- 20,001+ by April 30 would imply an extremely elevated pace, roughly equivalent to averaging 167+ arrivals/day over 120 days, which would be much higher than even 2025’s Jan-Jul average and would require extraordinary concentration into winter/early spring.

(d) Justification

Outside-view baseline:
- Since 2021, the UK has been in a high-arrival regime for small boats.
- Recent annual totals:
  - 2022: ~45.8k
  - 2023: ~29.4k
  - 2024: 36.8k
  - 2025: 41.5k
- That strongly suggests that very low early-2026 outcomes are less likely unless there has been a structural break.
- The available evidence does not show such a break in 2025; rather, 2025 was one of the highest years and had a record first half.

Operational/policy baseline:
- The “status quo if nothing changed” is continued substantial crossings.
- Returns under the UK-France arrangement were tiny relative to arrivals in the cited reports, suggesting little demonstrated capacity so far to dramatically compress totals.
- Political pressure is high, but political salience has been high for years without consistently producing large durable reductions.

Seasonal baseline:
- Jan-Apr is early enough in the year that totals should be well below annual pace extrapolations.
- Even in high-flow years, weather caps winter crossings.
- This points away from 20,001+ as the default outside view.

What would produce each option from an outside view?
- 0–5,000:
  - Requires either unusually severe weather for much of Jan-Apr, a step-change in French/UK interdiction, or major disruption of smuggling networks.
  - Historically possible in older low-flow years, but increasingly inconsistent with the 2021–2025 regime.
- 5,001–10,000:
  - Fits a meaningful decline from 2025 first-half pace or a weather-suppressed but still elevated year.
  - Plausible if 2026 reverts toward lower recent years.
- 10,001–20,000:
  - Best matches continuation of 2024–2025 conditions.
  - Broad enough to include moderate and fairly strong early-year accumulation.
- 20,001+:
  - Requires an exceptionally intense first four months, likely above recent seasonal norms.
  - Not impossible, but it would be an upset-level outcome.

Therefore, the outside view should center on 10,001–20,000, with 5,001–10,000 the main alternative. Very low and very high tails should remain materially possible, but clearly smaller.

Outside view calibration:
I want a distribution that reflects both the strong recent high-flow regime and the substantial weather-driven volatility in a January–April maritime route.

A useful tether:
- Recent analogous inferred Jan-Apr outcomes likely sit mostly in the upper 5k–10k to lower/mid 10k–20k space.
- Because 2025’s Jan-Jul total was nearly 20k, a continuation of that regime makes 10,001–20,000 more likely than not.
- But early-year volatility is nontrivial: a few stormy weeks or unusually effective disruption could knock the total back into 5,001–10,000.
- Conversely, 20,001+ by Apr. 30 would be a genuine outlier requiring extreme pace, so that should be low but not zero.
- 0–5,000 is even less likely given the entrenched post-2021 regime, but not impossible if there were a major unobserved crackdown or prolonged bad weather.

So I calibrate:
- Majority on 10,001–20,000.
- Significant minority on 5,001–10,000.
- Small tail on 20,001+.
- Smaller tail still on 0–5,000.
