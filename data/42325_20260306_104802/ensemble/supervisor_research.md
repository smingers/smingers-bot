### 1. Facts, Statistics, and Objective Measurements
*   **Regulatory Action:** The U.S. Department of Commerce’s Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) released a new regulatory framework governing the export of frontier AI technologies, including chips, cloud access, and AI model weights.
*   **Country Categorization:**
    *   **Exempted Allies:** 18 countries (including Australia, Canada, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and several European nations) are mostly exempt from these controls.
    *   **Countries of Concern (Country Group D:5 and Macau):** Includes China and Russia; these destinations are presumed ineligible for advanced U.S. AI technology.
    *   **Middle Category:** Includes all other countries (e.g., Saudi Arabia, UAE), which are subject to specific caps and licensing requirements.
*   **Chip Export Thresholds:**
    *   **General Exemption:** All countries (except those of concern) can import up to **1,700** of the most advanced chips (e.g., NVIDIA H100s) without counting against country-specific caps.
    *   **National Validated End Users (NVEU):** Non-U.S. headquartered companies meeting high security standards can purchase up to **320,000** controlled advanced chips over two years.
    *   **Non-NVEU Countries:** Countries without NVEU status are limited to importing **50,000** chips.
*   **Compute Base Requirements for U.S. UVEUs:** U.S.-headquartered Universal Validated End Users must maintain at least **50%** of their AI compute base in the U.S. and at least **75%** within the U.S. or close ally nations. No more than **7%** can be located in any other individual country.
*   **Model Weight Controls:** Licenses are required to export closed-weight AI models trained on more than **10^26 computational operations**.
*   **Open-Source Exception:** The policy does not control open-source models. The computational level of state-of-the-art open-source models will serve as the benchmark for future adjustments to closed-weight model regulations.
