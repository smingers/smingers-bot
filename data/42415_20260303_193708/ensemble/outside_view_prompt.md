## Summary: Hematopoietic Stem Cells (HSCs)

### Key Facts

- **Definition & Function:** HSCs are multipotent stem cells that give rise to all blood cell types through a process called haematopoiesis. The average person produces over **500 billion blood cells daily**.
- **Location in Adults:** Haematopoiesis primarily occurs in the **red bone marrow**.
- **Cell Types Produced:** HSCs give rise to myeloid cells (monocytes, macrophages, neutrophils, erythrocytes, platelets, etc.) and lymphoid cells (T cells, B cells, NK cells).

### Clinical Significance
- **HSC Transplantation (HSCT)** is used to treat blood cancers (leukemia, multiple myeloma), autoimmune diseases, and hereditary conditions. Sources of HSCs include bone marrow, peripheral blood, and umbilical cord blood.
- HSCT is described as a **dangerous procedure** reserved for life-threatening diseases, though survival rates have improved.
- Established therapeutic uses include chronic myeloid leukemia, acute leukemias, aplastic anemia, and hemoglobinopathies.

### Aging & DNA Damage
- DNA strand breaks **accumulate** in HSCs during aging, associated with reduced DNA repair capacity.
- Key repair pathway: **Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ)**; deficiencies in related proteins (Lig4, DNA polymerase mu, NHEJ1) lead to premature HSC aging and loss.
- Clonal diversity of HSCs **drastically reduces around age 70**.

### Research
- HSCs cannot be easily observed directly; behaviors are inferred through clonal studies and repopulation kinetics.
- Repopulation kinetics are heterogeneous but fall into **16 distinct groups**.
