### 1. Facts, Statistics, and Objective Measurements
*   **Definition and Purpose:** An IPO transforms a privately held company into a public one by selling shares to institutional and retail investors. Key objectives include raising new equity capital, monetizing investments for founders/private equity, and enabling easier future capital raising.
*   **Regulatory Filings (U.S.):** IPOs are regulated by the SEC under the Securities Act of 1933. The process involves filing a **Form S-1** (Registration Statement).
*   **The Prospectus:** Details of the offering are disclosed in a prospectus. A preliminary version, the "red herring," is used during the "quiet period" to gather indications of interest, though no sales can be made until the registration statement is effective.
*   **Underwriting Fees:** Lead underwriters (bookrunners) can take a gross spread of up to **8%** of the proceeds. This fee is split into a manager’s fee, an underwriting fee, and a selling concession.
*   **Greenshoe Option:** Issuers typically allow underwriters to increase the offering size by up to **15%** in the event of oversubscription (a "hot" issue).
*   **Historical Examples:** 
    *   **Theglobe.com (1998):** Priced at $9, peaked at $97 (1,000% increase), and closed at $63. The company raised $30 million but "left $200 million on the table" due to underpricing.
