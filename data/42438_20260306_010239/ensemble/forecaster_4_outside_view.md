(d) Justification

The outside view starts with this simple question: **What usually happens to teams with Duke’s likely seed/profile?**

The answer is: teams like this often make deep runs, but most still fail to win the title.

Duke’s current profile strongly implies a **No. 1 seed**, possibly the overall No. 1:
- No. 1 in AP
- No. 1 in NET
- nation-leading Q1 wins
- top ACC seed
- broad analyst/model support
- recent Final Four in 2025

That immediately moves Duke far above the generic tournament field. The most relevant historical base rates are seed-based. No. 1 seeds almost never lose in the first round (158-2 since 1985, per NCAA data), so “Round of 64” should be tiny. They also have by far the best odds to reach later rounds and win titles.

However, the base rate for a No. 1 seed **not** winning the championship is still much larger than the chance of winning it. Across the modern era, 26 titles by No. 1 seeds across 160 No. 1-seed entries implies roughly **16% title odds for a generic No. 1 seed**. Even if Duke is stronger than the average 1-seed, moving all the way into the 20s for title probability is already aggressive and should be done carefully.

A reasonable outside-view ladder is therefore:
- Near-certainty to survive Round of 64
- Strong chance to reach Sweet 16
- Meaningful but less than even chance to reach Final Four
- Roughly one-in-five or so chance to win the title, if one incorporates “stronger than average 1-seed” evidence
- More likely to lose before the title game than to reach it

The cautionary historical evidence matters:
- No. 1 overall seeds have not converted dominance into titles as often as the public expects.
- AP No. 1 teams are also not remotely close to title locks.
- March Madness is upset-prone by design, though 2025 was unusually chalky; one chalky tournament should not be over-extrapolated.

For an outside view, I therefore want a distribution centered on **deep run, but not title-favorite dominance**:
- Duke should be most likely to lose in the **Elite Eight or Final Four zone**
- A national-title win should be below a quarter
- Title-game loss somewhat below title-win or comparable depending on path assumptions
- Very early exits should remain real but modest

Outside view calibration:
The best anchor is the historical distribution for **No. 1 seeds**, with a small upward adjustment because current evidence suggests Duke may be **better than the average 1-seed**.

A rough historical shape for 1-seeds is approximately:
- very small Round of 64 loss rate (~1%)
- modest Round of 32 loss rate
- substantial Sweet 16 and Elite Eight exit mass
- Final Four around the low-40s to reach, meaning non-Final Four exits remain more likely than Final Four
- title around the mid-teens historically for generic 1-seeds

Given Duke’s especially strong pre-tournament profile, an outside-view calibrated uplift is warranted, but not a huge one. If generic 1-seed title odds are about 16%, and elite predictive metrics / overall No. 1 status can justify some uplift, then **~20% title odds** is a reasonable outside-view cap. Going much above that would lean too far into inside-view enthusiasm.

I also want to preserve realistic upset rates:
- Even elite teams lose before the Sweet 16 with nontrivial frequency.
- Tiny differences matter: e.g., 3% vs 1% for Round-of-64 loss is meaningful, but historical data strongly supports keeping this near 1%.
- Because the question splits championship-game outcomes into loss vs win, title-game-loss should also have healthy probability mass; it is easier to reach the final than to win it.

So I calibrate toward:
- **Early elimination (R64+R32):** low but not negligible, around 10%
- **Middle exits (Sweet 16+Elite Eight):** largest combined mass, around 40%+
- **Final Four but no title game:** meaningful
- **Title game appearances:** around low 20s combined
- **Championship win:** around one in five
