**(b) Reference class analysis**

I see four plausible reference classes:

1. **Short interstate crises involving direct U.S. military action against a regional state, with limited aims**
   - Examples: 1991 Gulf War (at least in its active combat phase), limited punitive strike episodes, 2025 Twelve-Day War as provided.
   - **Suitability:** High. This question is specifically about whether a formal bilateral ceasefire occurs within less than two months after open hostilities. Limited-war reference classes are especially relevant.
   - **Takeaway:** Such conflicts can terminate quickly if objectives are limited and both sides want to avoid expansion.

2. **Regime-change wars involving the U.S.**
   - Examples: Iraq 2003, Afghanistan 2001 in broader sense.
   - **Suitability:** Moderate. The background explicitly says the February 28 strikes had the stated aim of regime change, which makes this class relevant. But those wars usually involved invasion/occupation or long-term campaigns; current facts do not yet indicate a U.S. ground occupation.
   - **Takeaway:** If regime change is pursued seriously and the state apparatus fragments, a bilateral ceasefire becomes less likely because there may be no stable counterpart.

3. **Iran-related crisis diplomacy and de-escalation patterns**
   - Includes indirect U.S.-Iran confrontations, proxy escalation, the June 2025 conflict, attacks and retaliatory cycles where both sides avoided all-out war.
   - **Suitability:** Very high. Iran historically tends to calibrate responses; the U.S. also often prefers coercion plus off-ramps rather than prolonged direct war.
   - **Takeaway:** Baseline expectation should lean toward attempted de-escalation rather than indefinite sustained direct interstate war.

4. **Formal ceasefire agreements between hostile states reached within 1–2 months of conflict onset**
   - **Suitability:** High for the resolution criteria. The question is not “will fighting decrease,” but “will an official bilateral ceasefire go into effect.”
   - **Takeaway:** This is a narrower class and should reduce the rate relative to general de-escalation, because many conflicts stop or cool without a formal mutual ceasefire.

**Most suitable reference class:**  
A blend of **(1) short limited interstate crises** and **(3) Iran-related calibrated escalation/de-escalation**, adjusted downward by **(4) the requirement for a formal bilateral ceasefire**. I would use that as the primary outside-view frame, while using **(2)** as a cautionary tail risk because “regime change” rhetoric can lengthen conflicts or eliminate a coherent negotiating partner.

**(d) Justification for the outside view**

The outside view starts with the broad fact that direct U.S.-Iran military confrontation has historically been rare and usually bounded. Iran often seeks to avoid uncontrolled war, and the U.S. often prefers coercive demonstrations followed by diplomacy. That alone pushes away from expecting a long, open-ended direct war by default.

A particularly important base-rate anchor in the supplied material is the **June 2025 Twelve-Day War**, where:
- direct U.S. strikes occurred,
- Iran retaliated against a U.S. base in Qatar,
- and a formal ceasefire emerged quickly despite severe mutual hostility and immediate violations.

That is a strong, recent, same-theater precedent for quick conflict termination.

However, the current case is not simply a replay of June 2025. There are at least three negative adjustments for the outside view:

1. **The question requires a bilateral U.S.-Iran ceasefire, not an Israel-Iran ceasefire.**  
   June 2025’s formal ceasefire was primarily between Israel and Iran, with the U.S. as mediator/co-belligerent in part of the fighting. Here the resolution criterion is stricter: the U.S. and Iran themselves must mutually agree.

2. **The stated aim of the February 28, 2026 strikes was regime change.**  
   That increases the chance of fragmented authority in Tehran, hardliner resistance, or U.S. reluctance to formalize coexistence too quickly. Regime-change conflicts often do not transition neatly into bilateral ceasefires because the identity and authority of the counterpart are in flux.

3. **Formal ceasefires are rarer than informal de-escalation.**  
   The fine print excludes backchannel understandings, tacit pauses, and unilateral stand-downs. In many crises, that is exactly how violence ebbs. So the resolution criteria likely cut meaningfully below the probability of “substantial de-escalation.”

On the positive side:
- There were already **Oman-mediated negotiation channels**.
- Trump reportedly said the new Iranian leadership “wants to talk” and that he agreed.
- Both sides likely have incentives to stop short of prolonged regional escalation.
- Internal Iranian instability could increase the appeal of a formal halt to U.S. attacks.

On balance, the outside view should land in a **middle range**, not extremely low and not above even odds:
- **Not extremely low**, because recent same-theater precedent shows fast ceasefires are possible and there is a very short path from active strikes to diplomacy.
- **Not above 50%**, because bilateral formalization is a materially higher bar than mere de-escalation, and regime decapitation creates counterparty uncertainty.

My outside-view center is therefore around the high 30s to low 40s.
