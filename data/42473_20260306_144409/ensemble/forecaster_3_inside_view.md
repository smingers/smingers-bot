### (b) Evidence analysis

#### Strong evidence
1. **IRGC dominance in the immediate post-Khamenei environment**
- Multiple sources, including Reuters-derived reporting and several cross-confirming articles, say IRGC influence surged and commanders are pressuring the Assembly toward Mojtaba.
- This is a direct causal mechanism: the body with coercive power is trying to shape the vote.
- Effect: raises Mojtaba materially; also raises chances of a hardliner/security-aligned outcome generally.

2. **Hardliner-dominated Assembly of Experts**
- Structural factor from the outside view remains intact.
- Strongly disfavors Hassan Khomeini and other reformist or clearly moderate outcomes.
- Effect: lowers Hassan Khomeini versus any naive “national reconciliation” story.

3. **Institutional continuity under Article 111**
- Multiple sources confirm the interim council is functioning and meeting repeatedly despite war damage.
- This implies the state still has an operative succession mechanism.
- Effect: reduces “Position abolished”; keeps “None” limited though not negligible.

#### Moderate evidence
4. **Reported private shortlist from late Khamenei excluded Mojtaba and included Mohseni-Eje'i / Hassan Khomeini / Asghar Hijazi**
- From NYT reporting in background. If true, that matters as elite guidance and a focal point for constitutionalists.
- But Khamenei is dead, the war changed incentives, and Hijazi appears unavailable.
- Effect: boosts Mohseni-Eje'i somewhat; provides some residual support for Hassan Khomeini, though Assembly composition limits that.

5. **Resistance within Assembly to an overt dynastic succession**
- Reuters/JPost and Iran International claims mention boycotts, objections to hereditary rule, and Mojtaba’s lack of religious standing.
- Logical and historically resonant: father-to-son handoff jars with revolutionary anti-monarchical identity.
- Effect: caps Mojtaba; increases “Someone else,” especially another hardliner.

6. **Potential desire to delay formal appointment to avoid assassination risk**
- Reuters-cited possibility that naming a permanent leader creates a target.
- With ten months left, delay can matter for Jan 1 resolution if the war persists.
- Effect: slightly raises “None,” though still not huge because 10 months is long.

#### Weak evidence
7. **Trump’s public opposition to Mojtaba**
- Factually true that Trump opposes him.
- But his statements have uncertain causal impact inside Iran; they may even backfire.
- Effect: little direct adjustment.

8. **Claims Mojtaba was already selected**
- Too inconsistent and unconfirmed to rely on.
- Effect: only small upward nudge to Mojtaba, mostly because repeated rumors can reflect genuine momentum.

### (c) Timeframe analysis

Time to resolution: about 301 days, just under 10 months.

That is long relative to the expected pace of authoritarian elite succession. If the timeframe were halved to ~5 months, I would increase “None” somewhat and concentrate more probability on whichever candidate currently has coercive momentum—probably Mojtaba and Mohseni-Eje'i. If the timeframe were doubled to ~20 months, I would further reduce “None” and “Position abolished,” and increase “Someone else” because longer bargaining and institutional adaptation make compromise dark horses more likely.

### (d) Justification

Start from the outside view: hardliner-dominated Assembly, strong institutional incentives to fill the office, modest but real possibility of a surprise compromise figure.

Now adjust to the inside view.

**Why Mojtaba moves up**
- The single biggest new factor is the reported IRGC pressure campaign on behalf of Mojtaba. In a normal succession, hereditary concerns and limited clerical rank would hold him back sharply. In the current war-and-decitation context, control of the coercive apparatus matters more than normal.
- Several independent reports converge that Mojtaba is the IRGC favorite and the leading discussed candidate.
- That said, I do not move him to plurality by a huge margin because the objections are substantial: weak religious legitimacy, anti-dynastic taboo, and lack of formal office.

**Why Mohseni-Eje'i also moves up**
- He is on the interim council, is judiciary chief, is an Assembly insider, and was reportedly one of Khamenei’s private preferred successors.
- He fits the hardliner/security state profile without the same dynastic stigma as Mojtaba.
- If elites want continuity while avoiding a blatantly hereditary transition, he is among the cleanest solutions.
- This makes him, in my view, the most plausible single named compromise among regime insiders.

**Why Arafi is roughly steady/slightly down**
- Arafi benefits from being on the interim council and being a cleric with acceptable credentials.
- But recent reporting emphasizes the IRGC-Mojtaba push and Mohseni-Eje'i’s greater hardline institutional weight more than Arafi’s candidacy.
- He remains viable, but less focal than in the base rate.

**Why Hassan Khomeini moves down**
- Symbolic legitimacy is real, and he was reportedly on Khamenei’s private list.
- But nothing in the latest reporting suggests the hardliner Assembly or IRGC is rallying to a reformist-reconciliation figure.
- In wartime, with the regime under existential pressure, selection criteria likely shift toward security credibility and elite cohesion, not popular legitimacy.

**Why Ali Larijani and Sadeq Amoli Larijani remain live but secondary**
- Both are plausible elite alternatives if the top options deadlock.
- Ali Larijani has pragmatic credentials and may be useful if the regime seeks negotiations for survival.
- Sadeq has establishment credentials but less current visible momentum.
- The problem for both is that recent reporting centers the contest more around Mojtaba/IRGC pressure versus constitutionalist-clerical resistance.

**Why “Someone else” stays high**
- The 1989 precedent warns against overfitting to media-listed names.
- A secretive authoritarian elite under wartime stress may settle on an acceptable, lower-profile cleric.
- Evidence of factional deadlock and objections to Mojtaba push probability into compromise outsiders.
- I lower “Someone else” only slightly from the outside view because current reporting does point more than before to a narrower contest. But it remains a very large bucket.

**Why “None” rises a little**
- There are reports that formal selection may be delayed to reduce assassination risk.
- The Assembly itself is disrupted; meetings are online; wartime targeting is ongoing.
- Ten months is still a long time, so “None” should not be high, but it is somewhat more plausible than in a peacetime succession.

**Why “Position abolished” stays very low**
- Despite anti-regime protests and anti-velayat-e faqih sentiment, no reliable evidence shows a credible near-term constitutional path to abolishing the office by Jan 1, 2027.
- The current trend is institutional preservation under military-clerical control, not formal system redesign.
