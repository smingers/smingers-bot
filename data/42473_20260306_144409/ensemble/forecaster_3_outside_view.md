### (b) Reference class analysis

We need a reference class broad enough to have data, but close enough to be relevant.

#### Reference class 1: **Iranian Supreme Leader successions**
- Cases: essentially **one** prior real succession (1989), plus current interim mechanism.
- Strengths: perfect institutional match.
- Weaknesses: n=1 is too small for candidate-level inference.
- Usefulness: very high for structural questions:
  - a successor is likely selected rather than office abolished,
  - elite bargaining matters more than public legitimacy,
  - constitutional constraints can be bent to fit the chosen candidate.

#### Reference class 2: **Successions in entrenched one-party / authoritarian / revolutionary regimes after long-serving leader deaths**
Examples might include USSR, China pre- and post-Mao, North Korea, Baathist states, communist-party regimes.
- Strengths: larger sample; captures opaque elite bargaining under crisis.
- Weaknesses: Iran’s hybrid clerical-revolutionary constitutional system is unusual.
- Common pattern: successor usually comes from the **inner elite**, often from security/judicial/party organs; formal institutions ratify pre-negotiated bargains; radical institutional abolition is rare in the immediate aftermath.
- Usefulness: high for general base rates.

#### Reference class 3: **Theocratic or clerically filtered regimes selecting top leaders**
- Very small and heterogeneous.
- Strengths: emphasizes ideological legitimacy and religious credentials.
- Weaknesses: too sparse to estimate much.
- Usefulness: moderate only.

#### Reference class 4: **Leadership transitions during external war / military shock**
- Strengths: current case involves wartime assassination.
- Weaknesses: this cuts across too many regime types.
- Common pattern: strong pressure for continuity, security-heavy choices, and aversion to experimental reform during acute threat.
- Usefulness: moderate-to-high as an adjustment factor.

### Most suitable reference class
The best outside-view blend is:
1. **Iran 1989** for institutional mechanics;
2. **authoritarian revolutionary regime succession after founder/long-ruler death** for candidate-type base rates;
3. **wartime/crisis transition** as a modifier toward continuity and against abolition or reformist gamble.

This combination implies the baseline should favor:
- **some individual being installed** by Jan 1, 2027;
- that person likely being an **insider acceptable to hardline institutions**;
- meaningful but not dominant chance of an “unexpected consensus compromise” outsider from the named shortlist.

So the timeframe pushes two ways:
- **Toward a named permanent leader by Jan 1** because 10 months is ample time.
- But **not to certainty**, because no legal deadline plus war/assassination aftermath leaves room for “None” if the interim council persists all year.

Outside-view implication: “None by Jan 1” deserves some nontrivial probability, but much less than the combined probability of a formal appointment.

### (d) Justification for the outside view prediction

#### 1) Start with the status quo base rate
Status quo after Khamenei’s death is **temporary collective rule**, but the market explicitly says interim leaders do not count. So if nothing changes, the answer becomes **None** on Jan 1, 2027.

However, outside view says “nothing changes” is **not** the most typical path in a regime whose constitution and elite incentives are built around a supreme office. The Assembly exists largely to ratify continuity. In 1989 it chose an individual leader, not a council. Historical proposals to dilute or abolish the office repeatedly failed. That makes **Position abolished** a very low-base-rate outcome.

#### 2) The selectorate matters more than candidate popularity
The 2024 Assembly of Experts was hardliner-dominated after heavy vetting. That sharply reduces the outside-view odds of a genuinely reformist or system-softening choice. Public charisma, dynastic symbolism, or external approval matter less than elite acceptability to:
- senior clerics,
- security apparatus,
- IRGC-adjacent power centers,
- and institutional conservatives.

That generally favors:
- hardline judicial/security insiders,
- establishment clerics with regime credentials,
- compromise insiders.

#### 3) Crisis environments favor continuity and cohesion
Khamenei was killed in foreign airstrikes. In a wartime/national-security succession, authoritarian systems often prioritize:
- loyalty,
- institutional continuity,
- anti-fragmentation,
- and avoidance of ideologically disruptive choices.

That hurts:
- explicit reformist symbolism,
- constitutionally radical change,
- and potentially controversial dynastic succession if it risks internal backlash.

#### 4) But opaque successions often produce “someone else”
A key outside-view lesson from opaque authoritarian politics: public frontrunner lists are incomplete. Real outcomes are often compromise figures acceptable to rival blocs precisely because they were not overexposed. In 1989, Khamenei himself was not the obvious religious heavyweight. So “Someone else” should be sizable.

This is especially important because:
- there is only one prior Iranian succession;
- formal qualifications can be flexibly interpreted;
- hidden coalition bargaining can elevate a second-tier insider.

#### 5) Candidate-type base rates
Without doing inside-view candidate handicapping, the outside view should roughly rank option categories by institutional fit:

- **Insider hardline cleric on interim council / Assembly-linked establishment figure**: strong base-rate class.
  - Arafi and Mohseni-Eje'i fit this best.
- **Dynastic-security insider**: plausible but risky.
  - Mojtaba fits; dynastic succession is often powerful in closed regimes, but Iran’s anti-monarchical ideology creates unique resistance.
- **Symbolic founder-line reform-tinged cleric**: possible as a legitimacy reset, but weaker in a hardliner-controlled selectorate.
  - Hassan Khomeini fits.
- **Larijanis**: elite insiders, but less central in current succession reporting here; still not impossible as compromise candidates.
- **Someone else**: should remain material because opaque systems frequently surprise.
- **None**: meaningful due to no deadline and interim possibility.
- **Position abolished**: very low because it requires extreme institutional rupture.

#### 6) Distribution logic
Outside view should not overconcentrate on one named individual because:
- only one prior succession exists;
- the process is secretive;
- black-swan elite bargains are common in such systems.

So even the leading named candidate should probably stay well below 30% on pure outside view. The plurality should likely be spread across a few insider names plus “Someone else.”
