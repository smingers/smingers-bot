b) Reference-class analysis  
I tested three candidate classes:  
1. All modern U.S. shooting wars since 1990 that opened with large-scale airstrikes against a mid-sized regional power (Iraq 1991, Bosnia 1995, Kosovo 1999, Libya 2011).  4/4 ended with a formal cease-fire or surrender inside 80 days.  Pro: strategic similarities (air-dominant coercion, limited U.S. ground exposure).  Con: none involved Iran or an ideologically revolutionary regime.  
2. Conflicts in which the U.S. killed or deposed an adversary’s top leader mid-war (Germany 1945, Korea 1953 via Stalin, Libya 2011, Iraq 2003).  3/4 produced a cease-fire or surrender within 3–144 days (avg 52).  Iraq 2003 is the lone exception (no bilateral cease-fire).  Pro: mirrors Khamenei’s death.  
3. Historical U.S.–Iran kinetic episodes (1988 tanker war, 2020 Soleimani strike).  Base rate of reaching a formal cease-fire: 0/2.  Pro: actor-specific.  Con: very small N and none were wars with declared aims of regime change.

Class #2 captures both the decapitation precedent and a state-on-state war, so I use it for the base rate while noting the actor-specific drag from class #3.
