(b) Reference class analysis

I see at least four plausible reference classes:

1. Direct U.S.–Iran crisis episodes that could have escalated but de-escalated
- Examples: 2020 Soleimani episode; June 2025 Twelve-Day War; perhaps tanker/crisis episodes.
- Suitability: high, because the actors and strategic logic are directly relevant.
- Limitation: very small sample, and many episodes ended through tacit de-escalation rather than formal bilateral ceasefire. The current question specifically requires an official bilateral ceasefire, which is a stricter endpoint than simple calm.

2. Cases where the U.S. or a U.S.-led coalition killed or removed an adversary’s top leader during active hostilities, and hostilities then ended formally
- Examples: Hitler 1945, Gaddafi 2011, Stalin’s death as enabling the Korean armistice.
- Suitability: medium. Khamenei’s death is a major structural fact, so leadership-decapitation analogies matter.
- Limitation: these examples vary enormously in regime type, battlefield position, and legal structure of the war. Iran in 2026 is not Germany in total collapse, nor Libya in terminal regime disintegration.

3. Modern interstate or quasi-interstate wars ending in formal ceasefire within roughly 1–2 months
- Suitability: medium-low. Useful for time-to-agreement estimates.
- Limitation: many such wars do not involve the U.S.; many ceasefires are multilateral, informal, or not bilateral.

4. Crises with active Omani/Qatari/Gulf mediation and high oil-market sensitivity
- Suitability: medium. The Gulf’s mediation habit and U.S. preference to avoid prolonged oil shocks may raise ceasefire likelihood.
- Limitation: less systematic; more contextual than statistical.

Most suitable reference class:
- Primary: direct U.S.–Iran crisis episodes, especially June 2025.
- Secondary: leadership-decapitation cases where a formal settlement followed.
- Why: June 2025 gives the strongest actor-specific base rate that a short war can end quickly; the decapitation cases help adjust for the unusual fact that Khamenei has been killed.

What these reference classes suggest:
- U.S.–Iran crises often stop short of all-out prolonged war and can de-escalate fast.
- But formal bilateral ceasefires are rarer than de facto stand-downs.
- Khamenei’s death cuts in two directions:
  - positive for ceasefire if successors prioritize survival;
  - negative if succession chaos, nationalism, or hardliner competition blocks formal agreement.

- Median 8 days; mean 52 days, with the mean pulled up by the Korean outlier.
- This implies that if regime collapse dynamics dominate, a ceasefire by May 1 is quite plausible; if organized succession bargaining dominates, the window may be too short.

3. Ceasefire theory
- Preliminary ceasefires can be agreed quickly when trust is low, but durability is weaker.
- Since the question only asks whether an official bilateral ceasefire goes into effect, not whether it lasts, that lowers the bar somewhat. Even a shaky ceasefire counts.

So the timeframe is short but not implausibly short. There is enough time for a ceasefire if one is going to happen as an early crisis-management device. There may not be enough time if the conflict first passes through prolonged succession struggle, wider regional retaliation, or bargaining over nuclear conditions.

(d) Justification

For an outside view, I want to strip away some of the vivid details and ask: in analogous situations, how often do these conflicts produce a formal bilateral ceasefire within about two months?

The strongest single outside-view anchor is the June 2025 precedent:
- direct U.S. strikes on Iran,
- Iranian retaliation on a U.S. base,
- mediation via Gulf actors,
- quick ceasefire in less than two weeks.

That precedent strongly suggests that the U.S.–Iran dyad has a demonstrated capacity to move from direct strikes to formal ceasefire quickly when both sides perceive high escalation costs.

But this case is clearly more severe than June 2025:
- the U.S. itself says it is at “war”;
- Khamenei has been killed;
- U.S./Israeli strikes are framed partly around regime change;
- Iran has retaliated not only against Israel but against Gulf states hosting U.S. bases;
- domestic instability inside Iran is severe.

That severity cuts both ways. On outside view alone:
- More severe wars are often harder to stop quickly.
- But leadership loss and regime vulnerability can also accelerate ceasefire if successors seek breathing room.

The key outside-view tension is:
1. The actor-specific precedent says “quick ceasefire is very possible.”
2. The structural-severity precedent says “this could also become a messier, slower bargaining process.”

Another important outside-view consideration is the strict resolution criterion:
- It must be an official bilateral ceasefire between the U.S. and Iran.
- Informal de-escalation, indirect understandings, or unilateral pauses do not count.
This should push the probability down relative to generic “hostilities will calm” forecasts. In this region, tacit stand-downs are more common than clean legalistic bilateral declarations.

I also note the status quo if nothing changes:
- Continued hostilities or fluctuating strikes with mediated contacts in the background.
- Because “No” can result from many pathways—continued war, tacit de-escalation without formal deal, U.S.–Israel-only arrangement, Iran internal fragmentation, or a ceasefire after May 1—the no side has more routes.

Balancing these:
- Base rate from direct dyad precedent alone might suggest something near even odds or somewhat below.
- Adjustment for strict formality requirement and the unusually escalatory context should pull that down.
- Adjustment for Khamenei’s death and active mediation should pull it back up somewhat.

My outside-view conclusion is that a formal ceasefire before May 1 is more plausible than a generic peacetime observer might think, but still less likely than not.

Outside view calibration:
A reasonable tether from the sources is the leadership-decapitation timing distribution in the agent report:
- 3, 8, and 144 days, with mean about 52 days and median 8 days.
The deadline here is 56 days from March 6. On crude timing alone, two of three cited cases fit comfortably inside the window, one does not. That would suggest something superficially near 67%, but that is much too high because:
- tiny sample;
- poor comparability;
- Germany and Libya involved collapsing regimes and clearer endgame conditions than Iran likely has;
- the question requires a formal bilateral ceasefire, which is stricter than “combat ends.”

A second tether is the June 2025 precedent, where a ceasefire came in about 11 days. But using a single direct precedent as a base rate would also be overconfident.

So I would calibrate downward substantially from any naive 2-of-3 or 1-of-1 precedent count. Why?
- Upsets are common in crisis diplomacy.
- Formal agreements are rarer than informal de-escalation.
- In war termination, many conflicts that look ripe for settlement still overshoot expected deadlines due to domestic politics, face-saving, or verification disputes.

Putting this together, I’d place the outside-view probability in the low-40s: high enough to reflect the recent June 2025 precedent and the notional “ceasefire window” after leader decapitation, but below 50% because the formality requirement and multiplicity of No paths matter a lot.
