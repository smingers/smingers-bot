This summary focuses on the definitions, mechanisms, and historical precedents of ceasefires as described in the provided text, relevant to evaluating the potential for a formal agreement between the US and Iran.

### 1. Definitions and Technical Criteria
*   **Ceasefire vs. Other Terms:** A ceasefire (or truce) is a stoppage of war where both sides agree to suspend aggressive actions. Unlike a "cessation of hostilities," a ceasefire is generally intended to be **binding** and typically applies to the **entire geographical area** of a conflict.
*   **Formal vs. Informal:** While ceasefires can be informal understandings, they are often declared as part of a formal treaty or imposed by UN Security Council resolutions.
*   **Duration and Intent:** They can be temporary with a specific end date or intended to last indefinitely. They are distinct from an armistice, which is a formal end to a war.
*   **Bargaining Risks:** Actors may use ceasefires to re-arm or reposition forces, which can create "bargaining problems" that make implementation less likely.

### 2. Categorization of Agreements
The text cites research by Clayton and Sticher (1990–2019) regarding the success rates of different agreement types:
*   **Cessation of Hostilities:** Implemented quickly when trust is low; 30% remain violence-free after three months.
*   **Preliminary Ceasefire:** Stronger motivations to stop but trust remains low; 50% remain violence-free after three months.
*   **Definitive Ceasefire:** Part of a broad peace agreement (including disarmament); 70% remain violence-free after three months.

### 3. Historical Precedents and Mechanisms
*   **Korean War (1953):** Established via the Korean Armistice Agreement. Notably, the US and other principal belligerents signed, but South Korea did not, yet the ceasefire held at the 38th parallel.
*   **Vietnam War (1973):** A ceasefire was signed by all parties as a "prelude to the Paris Peace Accord" following a period of aerial bombing and draft peace proposals.
*   **Gulf War (1991):** Iraq and the UN signed a ceasefire agreement after Iraq was driven from Kuwait. However, because no peace treaty was signed, the "war remained in effect" for years, characterized by periodic US bombings (1993, 1996, 1998) in response to Iraqi actions.
*   **Israeli-Palestinian Conflict (2005/2023):** Agreements involved mutual declarations of "full cessation of violence" or negotiated "pauses" for specific humanitarian goals (hostage/prisoner exchanges).
*   **Global/Multilateral Efforts:** The 2020 global ceasefire appeal by the UN Secretary-General resulted in a Security Council resolution demanding a 90-day cessation of hostilities.
