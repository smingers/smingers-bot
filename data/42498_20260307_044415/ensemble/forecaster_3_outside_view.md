(d) Justification

The outside view starts from rarity. Supply-chain-risk exclusions of named firms under these authorities are rare, with only one known 41 U.S.C. § 4713 precedent mentioned (Acronis). That argues against overreacting to rhetoric and reminds us these designations are exceptional tools.

However, this is not a forecast from a cold start. Anthropic has already apparently been formally notified of a designation. So the relevant outside-view question becomes: once a formal exclusion order is announced against a firm, how often is it still in effect roughly two months later?

Status quo matters here. As of now, the status quo appears to be that Anthropic has been designated and that the administration wants the exclusion to bite immediately for new work, even while permitting a six-month operational wind-down. If nothing changed, Yes would be the natural resolution.

So what could force No by May 1?
- A court enjoins or stays the exclusion.
- The administration withdraws it.
- It turns out the purported designation was not actually a legally operative exclusion under either cited statute.
- Or the government reframes/reissues it in a way that leaves no currently effective qualifying exclusion on May 1.

From an outside-view perspective, the biggest risk to Yes is not political backtracking but legal interruption. This action appears highly unusual because:
- Anthropic is a domestic U.S. AI company, not a conventional foreign-linked supply-chain suspect.
- The publicly stated reason sounds less like sabotage/subversion risk and more like a dispute over acceptable military uses.
- Commentators across legal outlets quickly flagged apparent statutory and procedural weaknesses.
- The question’s fine print is strict: even a temporary injunction/stay on May 1 means No.

Still, outside view should resist taking legal pundit certainty at face value. Experts often overstate odds of immediate judicial intervention. Courts can move fast, but not always. Agencies can also compile some record after the fact, invoke urgent national security interests, or benefit from judicial caution where national-security claims are asserted.

Balancing these:
- The fact of an already-issued formal notice pushes well above 50%.
- The very short horizon favors persistence of the status quo.
- But the legal vulnerability and strict “not stayed/enjoined” criterion pull down materially.

My outside-view estimate is therefore modestly above even odds, not high confidence.
