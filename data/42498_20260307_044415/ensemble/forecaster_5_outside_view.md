Good parallel because: U.S. plaintiff, rapid suit, APA arbitrary-and-capricious standard, request for preliminary injunction.

Chosen reference class: #3 – “Domestic (or at least U.S.-based) firms that sue within weeks to block an executive national-security prohibition.”  This gives a meaningful sample for chances of a stay before a two-month deadline.

Empirical estimate from this class (approximate):

• Cases where ban still fully in force two months after issuance: ~40% (e.g., Kaspersky 2017, Keystone XL revocation 2021, some DoD JEDI protests).

• Cases where preliminary injunction or stay neutralised the ban within two months: ~60% (TikTok 2020, WeChat 2020, OTF 2020, Empire Wind 2025).

(d)  Justification

Base-rate (outside view) probability that an executive exclusion against a domestic tech firm survives un-stayed for two months ≈ 40%.  Adjustments:

+5 %  for the fact that this is the first time DoD used the tool on a U.S. company – courts may view as novel overreach (pushes towards reversal).

+5 %  because legal commentators across the spectrum say procedure was probably unlawful (lack of required risk assessment, congressional notice, written determination).  Historically, clear procedural defects raise injunction odds.

–10 %  because of the very short window: Anthropic has not yet even filed; they must draft, compile administrative-record objections, and hope the D.C. Circuit schedules an emergency panel quickly.  Any administrative “clean-up memo” DoD issues in the next few weeks could paper over some defects.

Net outside-view probability that the exclusion is still operative (i.e., question resolves Yes) = 40 % – 5 % – 5 % + 10 % = 40 %.

Outside view calibration:

• Anchor: roughly 40 % of analogous bans survive two months.
• Uncertainty: sample small; 1-sigma about ±10 percentage points.
• Upset frequency: courts occasionally defer to national-security claims even with shaky process (e.g., Kaspersky).  So we avoid over-confidence.
