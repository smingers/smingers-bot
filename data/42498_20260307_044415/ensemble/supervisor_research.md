This information is highly relevant to assessing whether a designation of Anthropic as a supply chain risk might be "enjoined or stayed" by May 1, 2026.

### 1. Facts and Legal Mechanisms for Emergency Relief
*   **Temporary Restraining Orders (TROs):** These are short-term orders (typically lasting no more than 14 days) designed to prevent "irreparable harm" before a court can hold a full hearing. Under Rule 65 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, a TRO can be issued *ex parte* (without hearing from the government), though courts often hear both sides.
*   **Preliminary Injunctions:** These can stay in place for months or years while a lawsuit is pending. Unlike a TRO, a court can only issue a preliminary injunction after the defendant has had a chance to argue against it.
*   **The "Irreparable Harm" Standard:** To obtain a stay, plaintiffs must prove that waiting for a final trial would result in harm that cannot be repaired by later financial compensation (e.g., the loss of trade secrets or the destruction of an irreplaceable asset).
*   **Likelihood of Success on the Merits:** For a preliminary injunction, the court evaluates whether the plaintiff is likely to win the case. If the plaintiff cannot show a high probability of success, the injunction is ordinarily denied.
*   **Balance of Equities and Public Interest:** Courts weigh the harm to the plaintiff against the harm to the defendant (the government) and the broader public interest.

### 2. Precedents and Opinions from Legal Contexts
*   **National Security Deference:** The article cites a 2008 Supreme Court case where the Court ruled that even if marine mammals were harmed by Navy sonar, the Navy's determination that the training was essential for national safety outweighed the environmental harm. This suggests courts may defer to the government on matters of military readiness.
*   **Success Against Executive Orders:** The article notes that in challenges to Trump administration orders (such as birthright citizenship), courts have issued TROs and preliminary injunctions within days of a filing. In those cases, courts found that the government has "no legitimate interest" in enforcing orders that are likely unconstitutional or exceed executive authority.
*   **Speed of Rulings:** The article highlights that in high-stakes cases against the administration, courts have issued "middle-of-the-night" orders and quick turnarounds to protect the status quo.
