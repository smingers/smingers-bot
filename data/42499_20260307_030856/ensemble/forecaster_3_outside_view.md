(d) Justification

The outside view should start from the empirical fact that the market has been sitting almost exactly on the 4.0M line for the past year. In the 12 monthly observations provided from February 2025 through January 2026, 10 of 12 months were at or above 4.0M, and only two were below: June 2025 at 3.98M and January 2026 at 3.91M. That naive base rate alone would imply something like an 83% chance of being at least 4.0M in a random month drawn from this recent regime.

However, that raw base rate needs some adjustment downward because:
- The latest observed point, January 2026, is below threshold.
- The 3-month and 6-month changes are negative.
- Sales were down y/y and weak across all regions.
- Low inventory remains a persistent structural brake.

On the other hand, there are offsetting reasons not to overreact to January:
- January’s 8.4% decline was unusually large relative to the recent monthly pattern.
- NAR’s named economist explicitly attributes at least part of the weakness to abnormal cold and precipitation.
- Affordability is reportedly the best since March 2022, with wages outpacing home-price growth and mortgage rates below year-ago levels.
- The hurdle for a “yes” is not demanding: from 3.91M to 4.00M requires only a 2.3% increase.

For an outside view, I should discount some of the narrative and focus on regime persistence. The recent regime is basically “sales around 4.0M, with noise of a few tenths.” In such a regime, and with only a short horizon to March, the base expectation is that March lands somewhere near the recent central tendency rather than far below it. The status quo if nothing major changes is therefore close to the threshold, slightly favoring yes because the center of recent mass is marginally above 4.0M.

A rough Fermi framing:
- If March follows the recent 12-month average, it is around 4.06M, comfortably above threshold.
- If January was mostly temporary weather noise, a partial recovery alone gets the series back over 4.0M.
- If underlying weakness dominates and inventory stays binding, the series could remain in the high-3.9M range.
- Since the threshold sits very near the center of the recent distribution, probability should be moderate-to-high, not extreme.

Outside view calibration:
Start with a simple empirical tether:
- In the 12 recent monthly observations provided, 10/12 were at or above 4.0M = 83.3%.

Then calibrate away from overconfidence:
- That 83% likely overstates the probability because it treats all months equally and ignores the recent downward drift into January.
- But a very low probability would also be unjustified because:
  - January is only 90k below threshold.
  - Existing-home sales often show sizable month-to-month noise.
  - The forecast horizon is short, which favors persistence near the recent mean rather than a dramatic further slide.
- In macro/housing data, “upsets” around a near-threshold binary happen often because one monthly print can be moved by weather, rates, closings timing, or sampling noise. So this should not be a 90%+ call.
- A reasonable calibrated outside-view range is roughly mid-60s to mid-70s.

I anchor near 70% because:
- Recent base rate says low-80s.
- Recent momentum says subtract meaningfully.
- Threshold proximity and possible weather rebound add some of that back.
- Short horizon and limited room for structural change argue against very aggressive adjustment.
