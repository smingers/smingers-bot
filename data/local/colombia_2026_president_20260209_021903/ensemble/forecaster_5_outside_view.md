(b) Reference class analysis  

Possible classes:  
1. All Colombian presidential elections since the 1991 constitution that required a first-round popular vote (1994–2022, eight cycles).  
2. Latin-American two-round presidential elections held 3–5 months after the measurement date.  
3. Any country’s elections where the incumbent party seeks but seldom wins a consecutive term.

Class 1 is preferred: same electoral system, similar partisan fragmentation, generally comparable campaign calendar.  It gives eight past data points—small but directly relevant.  

(c) Timeframe analysis  

Days until first round: 113.  Days until possible run-off: 134.  
In the eight prior cycles, the candidate ranked first or second in national polls taken 3–4 months before the first round has always reached the run-off, and seven of eight eventual winners were already in the top-three bracket at that time (the outlier is Álvaro Uribe in 2002, who was second but still top-three).  However, only two times (2002, 2018) did a candidate whose coalition had been out of power for 8 years or more lose the lead after February.  Continual volatility—e.g., Duque’s mid-March surge in 2018—shows that roughly a 30-40 % upset rate (defined as “not the February polling leader”) is normal.  

Incumbent-party advantage: Of the seven opportunities since 1994, the incumbent-backed candidate has won once (Santos in 2010).  Base rate for “continuity” ≈ 14 %.  

(d) Justification of outside-view baseline  

1. Structural two-person run-off means a non-fragmented bloc generally unseats a fragmented one.  Colombia’s left appears internally divided (Cepeda + Quintero/Barreras possibility) while the right could still coalesce (De la Espriella alone or plus Valencia).  Historically, the more unified camp has won five of the past six run-offs.  
2. Government fatigue: Petro’s net-18 pp disapproval echoes 2009–10 Uribe fatigue conditions that produced rotation.  That suggests a tilt against Cepeda.  
3. Poll standing on the measurement date: both Atlas and other pollsters put De la Espriella and Cepeda as the clear top two.  Fajardo hovers below the historical cut-line for run-off entry (<15 %).  Valencia is not yet a first-round candidate; unless she wins the 8 Mar conservative “consulta” her path is limited.  
4. Rate of late shocks: outsider surges (2018 Duque, 2022 Petro, 2022 Chile’s Boric, 2024 Panama’s Calvo) imply that giving a 15-25 % aggregate to the field beyond today’s top three is prudent.  

Outside view calibration:

1. Start with equal split among the two current leaders and “someone else.”  33/33/33.  
2. Apply continuity penalty to Cepeda, cutting his share by roughly half of the 14 % continuity base rate relative to leaders: 33 → 25 %.  Distribute the freed 8 pp proportionally to the anti-incumbent options.  
3. Apply “top-three requirement” base rate (7/8 ≈ 88 %) to limit long-shot share.  Allocate 12 % to “Another candidate,” leaving 88 % for the four listed names.  
4. Within the 88 %, assign 44 % to De la Espriella (slightly higher than Cepeda owing to anti-incumbent mood), 30 % to Cepeda, 10 % to Fajardo, 4 % to Valencia.  Check totals: 44 + 30 + 10 + 4 = 88 %.  Add 12 % for “Another.”  
5. Round to whole percentages and sanity-check against historical upset frequency (~30 %).  Our non-front-runner bucket (Fajardo + Valencia + Other) sums to 26 %, close to that yard-stick.
